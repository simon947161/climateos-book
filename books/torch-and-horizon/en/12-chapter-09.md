## Chapter 9: From Environmental Cognition to Possibility Computing
— CCZPS: A Cognitive Framework for Connecting Different Scales

The core question running through the previous chapters has always revolved around one word: understanding.

Understanding why systems fall out of balance. Understanding what we are truly trying to protect. Understanding why the future has become increasingly difficult to predict. Understanding why validation matters more than prediction. Understanding why we need to build a new environmental cognitive operating setting.

If ClimateOS seeks to address the problem of cognition, then the next question must be faced.

What comes after cognition?

This is a question even more difficult than cognition itself.

Because understanding does not automatically become action.

Seeing a problem does not mean being able to solve it.

Knowing that risk exists in the future does not mean society will necessarily change because of that knowledge.

In fact, the experience of human society over the past several decades has already proven this point.

Whether the issue is population, resources, the environment, or climate, a vast body of research has long existed. Predictive models, risk reports, and strategic plans have never stopped being produced. Many problems were not discovered today; they were pointed out decades ago.

The problem has never simply been a lack of cognition.

The problem is how cognition can be transformed into action.

More precisely, it is how cognition can be transformed into coordinated action across scales, regions, and actors.

In recent years, China has been comprehensively advancing its dual-carbon strategy. Across different regions, systems for carbon-emissions statistics, energy-use reporting, and the management of key energy-consuming entities have gradually been established. Taking Shanghai as an example, a large number of key enterprises have already been incorporated into energy and carbon-emissions management systems. Enterprises must continuously report data, while governments must continuously conduct statistics, assessments, and evaluations.

From the perspective of national governance, this is a highly important step forward.

It means that environmental issues are beginning to move from ideas into management systems, from advocacy into institutional systems, and from social consensus into concrete action.

Yet this also reveals a new problem.

When more and more data are collected, more and more indicators are established, and more and more management rules are implemented, the system itself begins to grow increasingly complex.

Enterprises see enterprise data.

Industries see industry data.

Cities see city data.

Provincial governments see regional data.

The nation sees macro-level data.

Every level is working hard.

Every level also has its own rationality.

But these levels are not naturally connected to one another.

Information can be uploaded.

Instructions can be issued downward.

But cognition does not necessarily become synchronized.

And so people begin to face a typical governance dilemma of the industrial age:

Local optimums continue to appear, while the overall optimum becomes harder and harder to achieve.

The problem does not lie in management itself.

It lies in scale.

Environmental problems are, by nature, cross-scale problems.

The carbon emissions of one enterprise may influence the energy structure of a city. The energy structure of one city may influence the development pattern of a region. The development pattern of one region may, in turn, influence the resource allocation of an entire nation.

If we continue tracing upward, these effects may also influence global energy flows, trade systems, and climate change.

At the same time, climate change flows back in the opposite direction, affecting regional rainfall, agricultural production, energy demand, and population migration.

This is a process of continuous circular feedback.

The management systems built in the industrial age are good at decomposing problems.

Yet the greatest feature of environmental problems is precisely that they cannot be fully decomposed.

This became one of the questions I kept returning to in my later thinking.

If environmental cognition has already been established, is there a framework that can connect the relationships among different scales?

Can there be a framework that understands the operating patterns of the Earth system upward, while also mapping downward onto the behavior of specific regions, specific industries, and specific actors?

Can it explain macro-level trends while also serving micro-level decisions?

This question eventually led me to propose CCZPS.

The original name of CCZPS was the Computable Climate Zoning Planning System.

But as my research deepened, I gradually realized that this name could no longer fully describe the task it was meant to carry.

Because the object it studies is not climate alone.

What it faces is the continuous interaction among environment, resources, energy, industry, population, and social activity.

Therefore, in essence, it is not a planning tool.

Nor is it a prediction software.

It is a framework for possibility computing.

Traditional prediction systems try to answer one question:

What will the future be like?

CCZPS tries to answer a different question:

What could the future become?

These two questions may appear close to each other, but in fact they represent two very different ways of thinking.

Prediction tends to search for a single answer.

Possibility computing acknowledges that the future contains multiple paths.

Prediction tries to reduce uncertainty.

Possibility computing tries to understand uncertainty.

Prediction focuses on outcomes.

Possibility computing focuses on conditions.

For this reason, CCZPS pays more attention to the internal driving forces of a system, rather than only to its final results.

These driving forces may come from energy flows, water-resource cycles, land-use change, and population migration. They may also come from larger-scale atmospheric circulation, ocean circulation, and the energy-exchange processes of the Earth system.

In other words, CCZPS is not concerned only with how the leaves tremble.

It is concerned with the wind, water, and sunlight that determine the direction in which the tree grows.

If ClimateOS addresses the problem of cognition, then what CCZPS seeks to address is the problem of scale.

It hopes to establish connections between the macro and the micro, between global change and local action, and between environmental systems and human society.

Only by understanding these connections can we truly understand the future.

And this is precisely the question to be discussed in the next chapter.

When we continue tracing things back toward their source, we will discover that many seemingly complex human problems eventually return to some extremely basic environmental dynamic processes.

Where does the wind come from?

Where does the rain come from?

How does heat move?

How do ocean currents change the fate of continents?

And how is civilization shaped by these forces?

From here, we begin to enter the true scientific foundation of CCZPS.
