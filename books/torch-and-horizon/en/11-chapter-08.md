## Chapter 8: ClimateOS: An Environmental Cognitive Framework

At this point, I can finally speak about ClimateOS.

But to be honest, it is no longer what I first imagined it to be.

Many years ago, when I began thinking about climate change, regional development, ecological restoration, and the future of humanity, I once believed that what we needed was a more powerful system. It would integrate more data, command greater computing power, connect more models, and ultimately help humanity predict the future.

Later, I gradually realized that this path might not truly hold.

Because the future itself is not an answer waiting to be revealed.

The future is more like a process that is always changing.

We may improve our ability to understand the future, but we cannot completely remove its uncertainty. We may move closer and closer to reality, but it is difficult to obtain a conclusion that is absolutely correct.

And so ClimateOS began to change.

It slowly moved away from being a system that tried to predict the future, and became instead a framework that helps us understand the future.

In this sense, ClimateOS is not a piece of software, nor a platform, nor a central-control system.

I would rather understand it as an environmental cognitive framework.

Or, more precisely:

An environmental cognitive operating setting.

What it seeks to answer is not what will definitely happen in the future. Rather, it seeks to help different people, different organizations, and future artificial intelligence systems gain a shared way of observing and learning when facing complex environments.

Over these years, I have come to believe more and more in one idea.

The environment is not merely one problem among many.

The environment is more like the common background within which all problems exist.

Economic activity takes place within the environment. Industrial development takes place within the environment. Urban construction takes place within the environment. Human civilization itself has gradually formed under environmental constraints.

If the environment changes, then the systems attached to it will inevitably change as well.

For this reason, ClimateOS does not try to begin from the economy. It does not try to begin from politics. Nor does it try to begin from any single industry.

It chooses to begin from the environment.

Because the environment is the shared reality faced by all actors.

No matter how nations compete, no matter how enterprises compete, no matter how different civilizations differ from one another, everyone ultimately still lives within the same Earth system.

And what ClimateOS is concerned with is precisely this shared reality.

To understand this reality, I have gradually summarized the whole framework into four continuous processes.

The first process I call “environmental facts.”

In the past, I often used the word Evidence. But later, I increasingly felt that the Chinese word “facts” is more accurate.

Because the environment does not change according to our preferences.

Temperature change is a fact.

Rainfall change is a fact.

Soil degradation is a fact.

Change in biodiversity is a fact.

These facts exist before opinions. They also exist before positions.

Without facts as a foundation, any discussion can easily fall into argument.

So the first step of ClimateOS is not to offer an answer, but to establish, as far as possible, a shared understanding of environmental facts.

The second process I call “cognitive validation.”

In the past, I used the word Validation.

Later, I discovered that its truly important meaning is not only to validate data, but to validate understanding.

Is our judgment about the environment correct?

Is our policy effective?

Is our technological pathway reasonable?

Have our actions truly improved the environment?

The essence of validation is to let cognition be tested by reality.

Because facts do not change according to our wishes.

If prediction is a kind of imagination directed toward the future, then validation is a form of humility before reality.

The third process I call “collaborative governance.”

In the past, I used the word Governance.

But the word governance can easily make people think of management and control.

In fact, what ClimateOS wants to express is not control.

It is collaboration.

Because fewer and fewer problems in the future can be solved by a single actor alone.

This is true of climate problems.

It is true of energy problems.

It is true of ecological problems.

It is also true of regional development problems.

Therefore, collaborative governance is not about finding the one correct answer. It is about helping different actors, on the basis of shared facts and shared validation, form mechanisms of action that can sustain cooperation over time.

The fourth process I call “continuous learning.”

In the past, I used the word Learning.

Today, I still believe this is the most important part of the entire framework.

Because neither human beings nor artificial intelligence can obtain the final answer all at once.

Any system that truly has life must have the capacity to learn.

Environmental change produces new facts.

New facts revise old cognition.

New cognition influences new decisions.

New decisions create new outcomes.

And so the whole system keeps circulating.

What ClimateOS truly seeks to build is precisely such a learning cycle.

Environmental facts.

Cognitive validation.

Collaborative governance.

Continuous learning.

And then, once again, a return to environmental facts.

It is not a straight-line process.

It is more like the breathing of a living system.

It was also in this process that the idea of EcoChain gradually emerged in my mind.

Because I discovered that the learning of civilization does not depend only on knowledge.

It also depends on memory.

Every person’s observation of the environment, every organization’s solution to a problem, and every region’s accumulated experience should not disappear easily.

If these experiences can be recorded, validated, and shared, then they can gradually become part of civilization’s common memory.

What EcoChain seeks to address is precisely the preservation of this memory, the recognition of contribution, and the inheritance of experience.

CCZPS, meanwhile, moves one step further on this foundation.

If ClimateOS is concerned with how we understand reality, then CCZPS is more concerned with how we explore possibility.

Reality is always singular.

The future, however, always contains many possibilities.

Different paths produce different outcomes.

Different choices form different futures.

What CCZPS tries to do is help humanity understand the differences among these possibilities, and continuously revise its judgment through validation and learning.

So when I look back at ClimateOS today, I no longer see it as an independent project.

It is more like an entrance.

An entrance through which humanity may rebuild its environmental cognition.

In the future, it may enter enterprise systems, city systems, education systems, AI systems, and even many more systems that we cannot yet imagine today.

But no matter how its form changes, its core purpose remains unchanged.

That purpose is to help different people, different organizations, and future artificial intelligence, when facing the same Earth, possess a shared capacity to understand the environment, validate reality, and learn from the future together.

Because what truly matters has never been ClimateOS itself.

What truly matters is whether humanity can still preserve the capacity for continuous learning.

And this, perhaps, is what most deserves to be protected in the intelligent age.
