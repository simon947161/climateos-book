## Chapter 7: Why Do We Need a New System?

At this point, many friends may ask a question.

If humanity already has governments, enterprises, research institutions, universities, the internet, and increasingly powerful artificial intelligence, why do we still need to discuss a new system?

Over these years, whether in China, the United States, or Australia, I have often encountered questions like this. Everyone is working hard, and everyone is trying to solve problems. Scientists study climate change. Engineers improve technology. Entrepreneurs create products. Governments make policies. Ordinary people try their best to live their lives. There has never been a lack of actors, nor has there ever been a lack of goodwill.

The real problem is that when problems become more and more complex, the organizational methods that once worked begin to struggle.

The industrial age built a highly successful management system. It was like a tree, with roots, trunk, branches, and leaves. Different departments handled different tasks. Different regions carried different responsibilities. Different industries solved different problems. This system helped humanity complete industrialization and created the prosperity of the past several centuries.

But the problems we face today are increasingly no longer single problems.

Climate change is not only an environmental issue; it is also tied to energy, industry, finance, trade, and social development. Population change is not only a demographic issue; it is also tied to education, housing, healthcare, and expectations about the future. Carbon reduction appears to be a technical issue, yet behind it lie regional development, industrial transformation, and international competition.

Problems now emerge across departments, across industries, and across regions, while most of our organizational systems still operate according to older patterns.

This is not anyone’s fault.

Systems of the past were designed for the problems of the past.

It is like the road system of a city. It may solve the problems of the automobile age very well. But when high-speed rail, drones, autonomous driving, and real-time logistics appear at the same time, the old logic of roads alone gradually becomes insufficient.

Over these years, I have come to feel more and more that what humanity truly lacks is not information.

Today, there is already too much information.

Every time we open our phones, we see news, data, and opinions from every corner of the world. Predictive models are also becoming more powerful: climate models, economic models, population models, and energy models appear one after another. Many future risks have long since been predicted, and many trends have long since been identified by professional institutions.

The problem is not that we cannot see the future.

The problem is that even after seeing the future, we still cannot form a shared understanding among ourselves.

An energy company sees energy security. A local government sees economic growth. An environmental organization sees ecological risk. An ordinary family sees the cost of living. What each person sees is a real part of the truth, but still only one part of the whole.

And so we begin to encounter a strange phenomenon.

Everyone is solving problems, yet the problems keep becoming more complex. Everyone is working hard, yet systems find it harder and harder to coordinate with one another. Everyone is producing data, yet very little of it becomes shared cognition.

Later, I gradually realized that the problem may not lie in the data itself, but in the absence of a mechanism capable of connecting different forms of understanding.

Human society in the past was more like a tree-like structure.

The greatest strength of a tree-like structure is stability. Its greatest weakness is slow feedback.

But living systems are not only trees.

The human brain, the nervous systems of animals, and even the exchange of information among plant roots all tell us something else: what truly keeps life alive is not structure itself, but the continuous feedback among structures.

Bones determine whether a living being can stand.

The nervous system determines whether it can sense the world.

Industrial civilization has already helped humanity build a vast skeleton. Nations, cities, enterprises, markets, laws, schools, and research institutions are all important parts of modern civilization. But as the world becomes more and more complex, we begin to need a new capacity.

This capacity is not management.

It is perception.

It is not control.

It is understanding.

It is not simply the execution of commands.

It is continuous learning and correction.

It was in this process that I began to think about a question.

If the society of the future will no longer be shaped only by human beings, but also by more and more artificial intelligence systems, digital systems, and automated decision-making mechanisms, then do we need a new shared language that allows different actors to understand one another’s positions?

This system is not meant to replace existing systems.

It does not replace government. It does not replace enterprises. It does not replace the market.

On the contrary, it hopes to help these originally scattered systems establish connections.

To allow the environment to be perceived, change to be recorded, action to be validated, and experience to be learned.

I later gave this idea the name ClimateOS.

But as my thinking deepened, I increasingly felt that it is not a piece of software, nor a platform, and certainly not a vast central-control system.

It is more like a cognitive framework.

A shared human cognitive framework whose basic coordinates are environment and climate.

Within this framework, environmental facts become the common foundation; cognitive validation becomes the common method; collaborative decision-making becomes the common process; and continuous learning becomes the common goal.

Environmental facts answer the question: what has happened?

Cognitive validation answers the question: have we understood it correctly?

Collaborative decision-making answers the question: what should we do next?

Continuous learning answers the question: how can this experience be transformed into future capacity?

These four processes are not isolated from one another.

The environment changes, and change becomes fact. Facts shape cognition, and cognition shapes decisions. Decisions produce action, and action once again affects the environment. The entire system becomes like a living body that learns through feedback, corrects itself through learning, and continuously improves its ability to understand the future through correction.

In this sense, ClimateOS is not designed to predict the future.

Nor is it designed to replace human thought.

It simply hopes to help more people participate in the building of the future.

Because the future should not belong only to a small number of experts, nor only to a small number of institutions.

Every change in the land observed by a farmer, every piece of equipment optimized by an engineer, every record of plant growth kept by a student, every dataset accumulated by an enterprise, and every environmental feeling held by a community may all become part of civilization learning about itself.

If the greatest wealth of the industrial age was energy, then the greatest wealth of the intelligent age may be cognition.

What ClimateOS attempts to do is simply to build this continuously growing cognitive capacity between people and the environment, between people and systems, and between people and the future.

Because only when more people can see their own position, see the positions of others, and see the position of the environment, can we truly understand this age.

And only then can we participate, together, in the building of the future.
