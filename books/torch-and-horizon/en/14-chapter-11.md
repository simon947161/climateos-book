## Chapter 11: The Meridians of the Earth: From Climate Dynamics to Civilizational Regulation

By this point, CCZPS is no longer merely a planning system, nor is it simply a tool for climate analysis. What it must truly face is a more fundamental question: how does the Earth system itself operate, and where, within this system, can humanity find its proper position of action?

If we imagine the Earth as a living body, then atmospheric circulation, ocean circulation, the water cycle, groundwater movement, and heat transfer are like its meridians. They are not always directly visible to human eyes, yet over long periods of time they determine the heat and cold, wetness and dryness, wind direction, rainfall, vegetation, and distribution of civilizations across the surface of the Earth. The droughts, floods, heat waves, cold surges, and bushfires we usually see are often only surface symptoms of changes taking place within these deeper flows.

The so-called Hadley circulation can be understood as a vast atmospheric cycle in which heated air rises near the equator, moves outward at high altitude toward both sides, and then descends in the subtropics. It helps explain why many deserts appear around twenty to thirty degrees north and south latitude. The Ferrel circulation lies in the mid-latitudes and forms part of the complex energy exchange between the tropics and the polar regions. The Walker circulation mainly occurs along the east-west direction of the equatorial Pacific, and is closely related to El Niño and La Niña, influencing rainfall patterns and climate anomalies around the world.

These concepts may appear to belong to meteorology, but they are not merely meteorological concepts. They determine where farmland can remain stable, where cities can expand, where water resources can accumulate, and where energy demand will rise. In other words, they are not a background. They are part of the underlying forces that shape the development of civilization.

The AMOC — the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation — is like a gigantic heat-transport system within the ocean. It carries heat from lower latitudes toward higher latitudes, helping maintain the climatic balance of the North Atlantic and surrounding regions. If this system weakens or behaves abnormally, the consequences may affect not only European weather, but also agriculture, energy, shipping, population migration, and geopolitics.

So what we are discussing is not mysticism, but something built upon foundations that modern science has already shown to be valid. For example, the classical continuity equation in fluid dynamics is:

$$
\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{u}) = 0
$$

In plain human language, this means that mass does not appear out of nowhere, nor does it disappear into nothing. It moves.

And this is precisely what the previous discussion has been pointing toward:

Wind flows. Water flows. Heat flows. Resources flow. Populations flow. Civilization, too, flows.

We may then introduce the principle of energy conservation:

$$
\Delta E = Q - W
$$

The change of any system is, in essence, the result of energy entering and leaving that system.

This is true of ecosystems. It is true of cities. It is true of nations. It is true of civilizations.

Therefore, what CCZPS truly cares about is not a single event, but dynamic relationships. A flood, a drought, or a heat wave, taken by itself, cannot explain the whole problem. The key question is whether these events arise from deeper systemic changes. If they do, then we must ask: along what pathways will such changes continue to transmit, and in which regions, industries, and groups of people will their consequences appear?

Here, a new way of understanding becomes necessary. In the past, we were accustomed to managing the world according to administrative boundaries drawn on the surface of the land. But the Earth system does not operate according to administrative boundaries. Wind does not stop at a national border. Water does not flow according to provincial lines. Heat does not obey an urban planning map. Human management boundaries are artificial, while the dynamic boundaries of nature arise from flow itself.

If traditional planning is more like arranging land on a map, then CCZPS is more like identifying key nodes within flows. What it seeks are the “acupoints” capable of influencing the direction of a system. This is somewhat like the meridians and acupuncture of traditional Chinese medicine, and also like the Chinese martial idea of using four ounces of force to move a thousand pounds. Truly skillful regulation is not about using enormous force to forcibly change everything. It is about understanding the operating patterns of a system, and then finding the positions where small interventions may produce large effects.

Climate governance is the same. Humanity has already affected the Earth system through carbon emissions, land development, energy consumption, and urban expansion. But we have not yet truly learned how to understand our own influence in a systemic way. We know about warming. We know about emissions reduction. We know about green electricity. We know about carbon accounting. Yet we still do not fully understand how these actions form long-term feedback with the atmosphere, the ocean, land, groundwater, and ecosystems.

This is the core point that Chapter 11 seeks to establish: the Earth is not a static map, but a dynamic living system. CCZPS does not seek to replace meteorological models, ocean models, or economic models. Rather, it seeks to establish dynamic connections among these models. It cares about how the surface, the underground, the sky, the ocean, and human activity transmit influence to one another, form feedback, and alter the possibilities of the future.

For this reason, the scientific foundation of CCZPS is not absolute certainty, but probabilistic dynamics. It acknowledges uncertainty. And precisely because it acknowledges uncertainty, it requires continuous observation, continuous validation, and continuous correction. As satellite remote sensing, ground sensors, AI models, digital twins, and computing power continue to improve, uncertainty will not disappear, but it can be gradually compressed, and the precision of understanding can continue to increase.

In this sense, what CCZPS truly wants to do is guide humanity from “seeing phenomena” toward “understanding meridians.” Seeing phenomena allows only emergency response. Understanding meridians makes regulation possible. Seeing floods, droughts, heat waves, and bushfires is only the first step. Understanding the flows, feedbacks, and dynamic structures behind them is the next step that future climate governance and civilizational governance must take.

If ClimateOS provides an environmental cognitive operating setting, then CCZPS provides the capacity for dynamic identification. The former helps us build shared cognition; the latter helps us find key nodes. One answers the question, “How do we see the world together?” The other answers the question, “How do we understand the way the world is flowing?”

This also opens a direction for the next stage of work. In the future, we need to place atmospheric circulation, ocean circulation, the water cycle, underground resource flows, energy flows, and human social activity into the same dynamic map for observation. Only in this way can we truly understand the meridians of the Earth system. And only in this way can humanity find its position for continuing forward without damaging the vitality of the whole.
