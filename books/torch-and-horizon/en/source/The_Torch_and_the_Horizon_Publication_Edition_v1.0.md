## Chapter 10: Can These Patterns Be Scientifically Described?
*— From Physical Laws to Earth System Dynamics*

10.1 Patterns Are Not Coincidences

By this point, we no longer need to prove that the Earth is changing. The previous chapters have already shown that expanding drylands, shifting climate zones, intensifying extreme weather, and changing ocean circulation are not isolated events. They are different expressions of the same Earth system operating across multiple spatial and temporal scales. The real question has therefore changed. If these patterns continue to appear repeatedly, are they merely coincidences, or do they reveal an underlying structure that can be described, validated, and eventually computed?

For centuries humanity has observed these phenomena. We recorded storms, rainfall, temperature, droughts and floods. Today satellites, remote sensing technologies, and supercomputers allow us to observe the Earth with unprecedented precision. Yet seeing more does not necessarily mean understanding more. Science progresses not by accumulating endless observations, but by discovering the relationships that repeatedly emerge behind seemingly unrelated events.

Looking back on my own journey, I realized that my travels across deserts were never simply about different landscapes. Whether in Xinjiang, Australia's dry interior, the Mediterranean basin or other semi-arid regions around the world, I was unconsciously following the same question. Different cultures and ecosystems shared remarkably similar environmental dynamics because they were connected by the same planetary circulation of energy, atmosphere and water.

This chapter therefore is not another introduction to climate phenomena. Instead, it asks a more fundamental question: Can these recurring patterns be described by a common scientific language? If so, could that language become the physical foundation of ClimateOS and the computational framework of CCZPS?

10.2 They Are Different Systems, but the Same Dynamics

Climate science often introduces the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC), the Walker Circulation, the Hadley Cell, subtropical high-pressure systems, monsoons, El Niño and La Niña as separate topics. Textbooks naturally divide them into different chapters because each possesses its own characteristics. However, after comparing drylands across continents, I gradually realized that they are not separate systems at all. They are different manifestations of the same planetary dynamics observed from different spatial scales and under different boundary conditions.

What changes is not the underlying physics but the environment in which that physics operates. Ocean currents emerge because seawater differs in temperature and salinity. Atmospheric circulation emerges because air differs in temperature and pressure. Wind interacts with mountains, forests and cities, continuously reshaping local weather. Although these systems appear independent, they all follow the same sequence: energy creates differences, differences generate gradients, gradients drive movement, movement redistributes energy, and the redistribution of energy establishes a new set of gradients.

From the perspective of ClimateOS, the objective is therefore not to predict the duration of a particular El Niño event or the exact arrival of a monsoon. The objective is to recognize the common dynamics shared by all these phenomena. Only after identifying their common structure can we establish a unified framework capable of connecting different regions, different disciplines and different scales of observation.

This is also why CCZPS is fundamentally different from conventional climate classifications. Rather than introducing another classification system, it seeks to organize the common dynamic architecture underlying all existing models. Models may continue to evolve and observations will certainly become more detailed, but the physical language connecting atmosphere, ocean, land, biosphere and human activity should remain unified.

10.3 The Earth Does Not Seek Stability, It Seeks Dynamic Balance

When we hear the word "balance," we often imagine stillness: a calm lake, a motionless rock, or an unchanging landscape. The Earth, however, has never been static. Oceans circulate continuously. The atmosphere moves relentlessly. Forests grow and decay. Ice sheets advance and retreat. Human societies transform landscapes at unprecedented speed. If stability meant the absence of movement, the Earth has never been stable.

What actually governs the Earth is dynamic equilibrium. Every day the Sun delivers enormous amounts of energy to our planet, while the Earth continuously radiates energy back into space. The equator receives more solar energy than the poles. Oceans store heat differently from continents. Water evaporates, condenses, precipitates and flows back to the sea. Every movement exists not to eliminate motion but to reduce differences within the system.

This fundamental principle has long been described by physics as the conservation of energy. Energy cannot be created or destroyed; it can only be transformed and redistributed. Wind, ocean circulation, vegetation growth, the carbon cycle and even human energy consumption all participate in this continuous redistribution process. Climate change is therefore not simply a collection of weather events. It represents a long-term adjustment in the Earth's energy balance.

$$
Q_{in}-Q_{out}=\Delta E
$$

Ultimately, ClimateOS is not designed to predict individual storms. It is designed to identify the forces that gradually alter dynamic equilibrium. A storm eventually ends. A flood eventually recedes. Even El Niño eventually weakens. But if the underlying energy gradients continue to evolve, new events will inevitably emerge.

10.4 Mass Is Conserved

If energy is continuously redistributed, another equally fundamental question arises: what exactly is being moved? Air does not appear from nowhere. Water does not disappear without trace. Carbon, nutrients, sediments and moisture are constantly transferred between oceans, atmosphere, land and living organisms. They change form and location, but they remain subject to one of the most fundamental principles of nature: mass is conserved.

The continuity equation expresses this simple but profound idea.

$$
\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}+\nabla\cdot(\rho\mathbf{u})=0
$$

Although the equation appears abstract, its meaning is remarkably intuitive. Any increase in mass within a region must be explained by inflow exceeding outflow. Likewise, any decrease must result from more material leaving than entering. Wind, rivers, ocean currents and atmospheric circulation all obey this same relationship.

For ClimateOS, the significance of the continuity equation extends beyond mathematics. It reminds us that no environmental phenomenon exists in isolation. A drought in one region often corresponds to excessive rainfall elsewhere. Carbon stored in forests must originate from the atmosphere. Ocean circulation weakens not because water disappears, but because the pathways of mass transport change. Every local observation belongs to a larger planetary continuity.

10.5 The Earth Moves Because Differences Exist

If continuity explains why matter is conserved, another question naturally follows: why does anything move at all? The answer lies in one word: difference. The Earth is fundamentally heterogeneous. Solar radiation is unevenly distributed between the equator and the poles. Land heats faster than oceans. Mountains differ from plains. Temperature, pressure, humidity and salinity vary continuously across space. These differences create gradients, and gradients generate movement.

The Earth therefore behaves as an open system that continuously reduces imbalance rather than seeking complete uniformity. Heat flows from warmer regions to cooler ones. Air moves from higher pressure to lower pressure. Water flows downhill under gravity. Ocean currents redistribute density differences. Every circulation is an expression of gradients attempting to reorganize the Earth's energy and material distribution.

This is why wind should not be understood merely as a weather phenomenon, nor ocean circulation as water moving by chance. Wind is the visible expression of pressure gradients. Ocean circulation reflects temperature, salinity and density gradients. Monsoon systems emerge from long-term thermal contrasts between land and sea. What appears to be weather at the surface is often the expression of deeper differences within the Earth system.

CCZPS emerged from this realization. Rather than classifying climates, it seeks to organize evolving gradient fields. Temperature, humidity, wind, ocean circulation, vegetation and human activity become interconnected components of one continuously changing system. The purpose is not merely to name climate zones, but to understand how differences form, move, accumulate and transform into new possibilities.

10.6 Mathematics Describes Relationships, Not Reality

Mathematics often gives us a comforting sense of certainty. Equations appear precise, elegant and complete. Yet the Earth itself is never an equation. Mathematics does not replace reality; it provides a language through which relationships within reality become visible. A formula cannot replace wind, ocean, forest, soil or the long environmental memory of a region.

This observation should not diminish the value of mathematics. On the contrary, mathematical descriptions allow us to compare systems, identify recurring structures and test competing hypotheses. Continuity describes conservation. Thermodynamics describes energy transfer. Fluid dynamics describes movement. Each equation illuminates one relationship, but none of them alone represents the Earth itself.

This distinction is central to ClimateOS. Models describe possible realities, but they are not reality. Equations express relationships, but those relationships must continuously be confronted with observations. Scientific progress therefore does not arise from believing models unconditionally. It emerges through continuous validation, correction and learning.

Therefore, mathematics serves here as a bridge. It connects phenomena with patterns, observations with models, and local experience with system-level understanding. It allows a storm, a drought, an ocean-current anomaly or a degrading forest to be placed within a larger network of relationships. But it also reminds us that every formula must eventually return to reality and accept correction from reality.

From this perspective, CCZPS does not attempt to reduce the Earth to formulas. It uses formulas to help us understand the relationships that continuously occur within Earth systems. ClimateOS does not ask mathematics to decide the future for humanity. It asks mathematics, observation, experience and validation to work together in judging the future more responsibly.

10.7 From Equations to ClimateOS

By this stage, several classical equations have appeared. The continuity equation describes the conservation of matter. The Navier–Stokes equations describe fluid motion. Energy conservation explains climate as a redistribution of energy. Wave propagation reveals how disturbances travel across the Earth system. The ideal gas law connects temperature, pressure and atmospheric behavior. Together they form part of the scientific language through which we understand planetary processes.

$$
\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}+\nabla\cdot(\rho\mathbf{u})=0
$$

Mass is never created from nothing, nor does it disappear without consequence. It only moves, transforms and redistributes.

$$
\rho\left(\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t}+\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla\mathbf{u}\right)
=-\nabla p+\mu\nabla^2\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{f}
$$

Air, rivers and oceans all move according to the balance between forces and resistance. Their forms differ, but their movement can be described through the same underlying physical logic.

$$
Q_{in}-Q_{out}=\Delta E
$$

Climate is fundamentally the redistribution of energy. Warming, cooling, rainfall, drought, melting and circulation all reveal changing pathways of energy storage and release.

$$
v=f\lambda
$$

Many climate oscillations propagate as waves through the Earth system. Disturbances do not remain fixed in one place; they travel, resonate and reshape distant regions over time.

$$
PV=nRT
$$

Temperature, pressure and atmospheric behavior are fundamentally linked. This simple relationship reminds us that the atmosphere is not an abstract background, but a physical system constrained by measurable relationships.

Yet these equations are not the destination. They are the foundation. ClimateOS does not attempt to replace physics, nor does it seek to construct an entirely new Earth system model. Instead, it connects existing scientific knowledge, observational systems, predictive models and future artificial intelligence into a continuously evolving framework of cognition, validation and learning.

The equations themselves do not govern the Earth. They describe how the Earth behaves. What ultimately governs the future is how humanity learns from those descriptions.

方程本身并不支配地球，它们只是描述地球如何运行；真正决定未来的，是人类如何从这些描述中持续学习。

It is precisely here that ClimateOS begins. Not as another prediction system, but as an Earth System Validation Runtime, a framework that continuously connects observation, prediction, validation, governance and learning. The purpose is not merely to understand how the Earth works, but to help humanity make wiser decisions while remaining part of the very system it seeks to understand.

## Abstract

Over the past century, humanity has built increasingly powerful systems for observing and predicting the Earth.

From meteorological stations and remote-sensing satellites to numerical weather prediction, Earth system models, digital twins, and artificial intelligence, humanity’s ability to simulate the future is growing at an unprecedented speed.

However, since entering the twenty-first century, the core problem we face has begun to change.

The problem is no longer simply a lack of data, nor merely a lack of models. Rather, when more and more data, models, digital twins, and artificial intelligence systems exist at the same time, how can humanity judge which results are worthy of trust, and what actions should be taken based on them?

This book argues that the key to the future development of Earth system intelligence is not only to predict the future, but more importantly, to validate the future.

Based on the author’s more than twenty years of practice and reflection in environmental planning, landscape design, regional development, ecological governance, and climate adaptation, and combined with recent explorations of ClimateOS, CCZPS — the Computable Climate Zoning Planning System — Validation Runtime, and Possibility Computing, this book attempts to construct a new cognitive framework.

This framework does not attempt to build a larger Earth model, nor does it seek to replace existing climate prediction systems. Instead, it adds a layer of continuous validation, continuous correction, and continuous learning between observation, prediction, digital twins, and governance decisions.

This book proposes that the core of future governance capacity lies not in possessing more models, but in establishing validation models; not in obtaining more information, but in forming trusted cognition; not in pursuing a single answer, but in understanding the relationships among multiple possible futures.

Therefore, ClimateOS is not merely a technical system, but also an environmental cognitive framework. CCZPS is not merely a planning tool, but also a method for understanding the complex relationships among environment, society, and civilization.

From climate change to ecological restoration, from the dual-carbon transition to artificial intelligence, from environmental governance to Earth system governance, this book attempts to answer one question that runs through all of its discussions:

When the future becomes increasingly uncertain, how can humanity maintain a sense of direction and continue to create futures worth reaching?

This is both the starting point of ClimateOS and the central proposition toward which all discussions in this book ultimately point.

Why Am I Writing This Book?

Many friends have asked me the same question:

Why spend so many years thinking about a question that seems to have almost no end?

To be honest, I do not have a grand answer.

I am not a climate scientist, nor am I an artificial intelligence expert. I am certainly not someone who can decide the direction of global governance. I am simply a practitioner who has long worked in environmental planning, ecological construction, and regional development. Over the past twenty-plus years, I have worked on cities, villages, industrial parks, and river basins. I have also traveled through the deserts of northwestern China, the coastal cities of eastern China, the arid regions of the American West, and the vast landscapes of Australia.

These experiences did not give me answers directly. Instead, they kept bringing me back to the same question.

Why can some places prosper over the long term, while others gradually decline? Why can the same method succeed in one place, yet fail in another? Why does humanity possess increasingly advanced technologies, yet still face similar environmental and development dilemmas again and again?

I have increasingly come to realize that what is truly worth understanding may not be a particular technology or a particular policy, but the long-existing connections among environment, resources, society, and civilization — connections that are often overlooked.

Many people see this generation of Chinese people who have gone out into the world as part of globalization.

I did indeed leave China against the backdrop of that era, continuing my studies, work, and life overseas.

But I increasingly feel that this experience cannot simply be summarized as “immigration.”

If we take a longer view of time, I would rather see myself as an ordinary member of the countless Chinese explorers who, since modern times, have gone out into the world.

More than a century ago, many people crossed the oceans in search of paths toward modern science, industry, and education. After reform and opening up, generation after generation of Chinese people went out into the world, learning and communicating across different civilizations, while also rediscovering China and the world.

I am only a very ordinary one among them.

After coming overseas, I certainly saw many things worth learning from. Mature institutions, long-accumulated scientific traditions, and open international cooperation all benefited me greatly.

At the same time, however, I gradually discovered that today’s world is no longer merely a question of “whom we should learn from.”

Every civilization has its own achievements, and every civilization has its own limitations. Developed countries have not solved all problems, and China still has many areas that require continued exploration. What truly matters is not who replaces whom, but who can continue learning in a new era.

These experiences have made me increasingly believe that I did not come overseas to search for a ready-made answer.

I am more like someone who keeps comparing, observing, and validating across different civilizations, hoping to understand those patterns that remain true across cultures, countries, and eras.

Western science helped me understand the important value of analysis, validation, and professional specialization. At the same time, the holistic thinking, relational thinking, and long-formed idea of interdependence between humanity and nature in Eastern traditions have also led me to reconsider whether there may be another possibility for the development of civilization.

Later, I gradually discovered that what I was truly seeking was not the so-called “scriptures from the West.”

What I truly brought back was not a particular technology, nor a particular institution.

Rather, it was the ability to raise new questions again, through the collision and comparison of different civilizations.

Perhaps what humanity truly needs is not for one civilization to defeat another, nor a new form of zero-sum competition. Instead, as we jointly face the environment, resources, artificial intelligence, and the future, we need to reconnect the wisdom that different civilizations have accumulated over long periods of time.

After coming to Australia, this line of thought did not stop.

I have participated in planning and design, and I have also done ordinary physical labor. I have worked in a timber mill, assisted with teaching in schools, and continued to take part in community and environment-related practices. During the day, I face real life; at night, I continue revising this book. This contrast has not made me give up thinking. On the contrary, it has made me believe even more that a truly valuable idea must be able to return to ordinary people’s lives and face the real world, rather than remain only on paper.

In recent years, the development of artificial intelligence has given me new inspiration.

In the past, knowledge that took humanity decades to accumulate is now being connected at an unprecedented speed. I have begun to realize that we may be standing at the threshold of a new era. What will truly matter in the future is not merely making artificial intelligence smarter, but helping humanity and artificial intelligence jointly build a capacity for continuous observation, continuous validation, and continuous learning.

This is exactly why I wrote The Torch and the Horizon.

I do not believe that ClimateOS is already mature, nor do I believe that every view in this book has already been proven. On the contrary, it is more like an open record of thought, an exploration still in progress. I hope it can become a torch rather than a destination; a starting point for more discussion rather than the final answer.

If, one day, many of the ideas in this book are proven wrong, I will also be happy. Because what truly matters has never been the defense of a particular theory, but humanity’s continued movement toward reality.

Perhaps, decades from now, people will not remember ClimateOS, nor will they remember my name.

But if people in the future can understand the environment better than we do today, understand one another better, and understand the Earth we share more deeply; if modern science, artificial intelligence, and the long-accumulated wisdom of different civilizations can form new connections because of this era, then this book will have fulfilled its mission.

This book is not written for those who already have answers today. It is written for those who are still willing to keep asking questions.

Because the true torch of civilization is not the answer, but the courage to continue searching for answers.

The horizon represents a future not yet reached. The torch represents the questioning consciousness and truth-seeking spirit that continue to illuminate the road ahead.

### The First Step of ClimateOS

Many people, when they first hear about ClimateOS, ask the same question:

If the goal is to serve global environmental governance, where should we begin?

The answer is not to begin with the globe.

ClimateOS has never been a system that can only operate at the national or international level. On the contrary, it can begin in any place that is willing to improve environmental cognition and accumulate real evidence.

It could be a community, a campus, an industrial park, a river, a watershed, or even a specific engineering project.

ClimateOS does not require complete data at the beginning, nor does it require solving every problem all at once. Its first step is simply to establish an environmental cognitive process of continuous observation, continuous validation, and continuous learning. Every real observation, every validation result, and every correction of experience will become a more reliable foundation for the next decision.

As practice continues to accumulate, individual projects can be connected into communities; communities can be connected into cities; cities can be connected into regions; and regions can eventually form collaborative governance networks across countries, watersheds, and ecosystems.

Therefore, the growth path of ClimateOS is not from the top down, but from points to surfaces, from the local to the whole, and from practice to civilization.

```text
Individual Project
↓
Community
↓
Industrial Park / Watershed
↓
City
↓
Region
↓
National Collaboration
↓
Global Environmental Governance (EarthOS)
```

What truly changes the world is never the creation of a massive system from the very beginning. It is enabling countless small but real practices to connect with one another, learn together, and continuously accumulate trusted evidence.

The first step of ClimateOS is not to build a platform that covers the entire globe, but to light the first torch in any place willing to begin.

Because every torch can illuminate a road farther ahead, and every real practice can become part of the shared learning of future civilization.

## Chapter 1: Why Do Some Systems Fall Out of Balance?
— From Extreme Weather to the Vulnerability of Civilization

This summer, I saw news reports about a series of extreme weather events across China. Northeastern China experienced unusually low temperatures and severe storms, while parts of southern China suffered from persistent heat waves and heavy rainfall. For people today, extreme weather no longer seems unfamiliar. Yet when these phenomena appear in the news more and more frequently, I still cannot help but pause and think. Low temperatures in the Northeast, droughts in inland regions, heavy rainfall along the coast, bushfires in Australia, heat waves in Europe, and the constant fluctuations in food prices, energy supply, and insurance costs over recent years may appear to belong to different countries and different fields. Yet at some level, they seem to be connected.

Many people group these phenomena under the term climate change. But I increasingly feel that climate change may only be the surface expression. The deeper problem may come from the system itself.

This line of thought did not begin recently. Looking back, I seem to have been asking this question for more than twenty years. When I was young, I did not know what I was looking for. I thought I was studying design. Later, I thought I was studying cities. Later still, I thought I was studying ecology and climate. Only after many years did I slowly realize that what I had been observing all along was how systems form, and why they fall out of balance.

In 2005, I moved from Shanghai to Shenzhen for work. At that time, Shenzhen was developing at a very rapid pace. The whole industry was learning from southern experience, and many design methods were regarded as generally applicable. But very soon, I discovered a very real contradiction in project work: the same design scheme could work in the South, yet might not work at all in the North.

At that time, we had already clearly proposed the concept of “north-south differences.” Plants were different. Spaces were different. Climate was different. Even people’s habits of use and ways of life were different. Many small gardens and small-scale landscapes that could be easily realized in Shenzhen produced completely different effects when placed in northern China. It was then that I began to realize that a method successful in one place could not necessarily be copied to another. The environment itself has its own logic, and the first responsibility of a designer is to respect that logic.

Looking back today, this may seem like only a professional and technical issue. But at the time, it had already planted a line of thought that would influence me for many years afterward. If plants change because of the environment, and if design methods fail because of regional conditions, then could cities, industries, and even civilizations also face similar problems? Could some of the success stories we take for granted only apply under specific conditions, and therefore cannot be replicated without limit?

Around 2008, I had returned to Shanghai and begun participating in larger-scale municipal projects and work related to the World Expo. It was also during that period that I first truly encountered the issue of climate change. Discussions about sea-level rise, extreme weather, and global warming were becoming more frequent, and many experts were predicting the risks that might appear in the future.

To be honest, at that time I did not believe I could solve a problem as vast as climate change. I simply felt that if this was a challenge all of humanity would face over the coming decades, then someone should try to understand it, study it, and seek an entry point from the field they knew best. I have always believed that major problems do not necessarily have to be solved from the center; breakthroughs may also emerge from the margins.

So I began to think about another question. If sea levels really continued to rise, what would happen inside the Eurasian continent? When coastal cities came under pressure, would inland regions face new opportunities and new problems? With these questions in mind, I turned my attention to Xinjiang, to those arid regions that rarely receive attention from the mainstream perspective.

Later, I went to Xinjiang. What attracted me at first was not the project itself, but the question. I wanted to know what would happen, under the broader background of climate change, to those regions far from the ocean and far from mainstream attention. Many people were focused on coastlines, but I wanted to look at the center of the continent. Many people were discussing future risks, but I wanted to look at future possibilities.

There, I participated in planning research in places such as Karamay, and I also came into contact with many materials on arid-region ecosystems. The research findings of the Institute of Arid Ecology at Xinjiang University gave me great inspiration. For the first time, I systematically learned the causes of saline-alkali land formation and began to understand the complex relationships among precipitation, evaporation, groundwater, and soil. Many things that had been taken for granted in coastal areas became entirely different problems in arid regions.

It was then that I began to realize that many so-called development problems are, first of all, environmental problems. Why does land degrade? Why does vegetation disappear? Why do rivers change course? These changes do not happen suddenly on a single day. They accumulate little by little over decades, or even over centuries. The truly dangerous part of a system often does not lie in sudden collapse, but in the fact that when it has already begun to fall out of balance, people still think everything is normal.

In search of more answers, I later went to the United States.

Many Chinese people come to know America through New York, Wall Street, or Silicon Valley. But what interested me most were the arid regions of the American West. I went to Arizona, Nevada, and California, and I also observed the development patterns around Los Angeles and Las Vegas.

There, I truly felt for the first time the shaping power of infrastructure in the development of civilization. Many Chinese cities are accustomed to expanding outward from a central urban core, with ring roads growing outward one after another. Many cities on the American West Coast, however, unfold along transportation corridors, water resources, and mountain boundaries, forming a pattern of linear development. Where there is a stable water source, communities are more likely to form. Where ecological conditions are better, wealth is more likely to accumulate. Behind many issues that appear to be economic, there are still ecological and resource-related questions.

My trip to the United States led me to think about another question. Where does the prosperity of a civilization truly come from? Does it come from capital? From institutions? From technology? Or from those deeper natural conditions and infrastructures that are often overlooked? I had no answers to these questions at the time, but they stayed in my mind.

In 2015, I came to Australia.

If the United States allowed me to see how resources support the development of a civilization, Australia allowed me to see another challenge faced by a successful civilization. This country has abundant mineral resources, stable social institutions, and a relatively high standard of living. Many people long for such a life, and I did indeed see aspects of this country that deserve respect.

At the same time, however, I also saw a kind of inertia. The comfort brought by resources made many people believe that their existing way of life could continue for a long time. Mining, energy, and export industries support the functioning of the economy. People enjoy a high quality of life, yet without realizing it, they may also lose part of the motivation to change the future.

Once, I discussed future resource issues with an Australian scientist. He told me that, according to existing research, Australia’s underground water reserves would be sufficient for humanity to use for thousands of years. His judgment was not wrong, but I did not immediately feel optimistic.

I suddenly thought of the film City of Ember. In that story, humanity is forced to move underground because the surface environment has deteriorated. More than two hundred years later, people have even forgotten that they once came from the surface. So I asked him: if we can keep the surface ecosystem healthy, keep rivers flowing, keep forests growing, and allow people to continue living under the sunlight, then why must we go underground to live?

For many years afterward, I remembered that conversation. Because I discovered that when human beings face environmental problems, the discussion is often about whether resources are sufficient. But what I care about more is another question: what kind of future do we ultimately hope to build?

Only many years later, when I looked back again, did I realize that what I had been asking was not really about weather, nor only about climate change. What I truly wanted to understand was why some systems can maintain vitality over the long term, while others slowly move toward imbalance.

The storms in Harbin, the plants in Shenzhen, the saline-alkali land in Xinjiang, the infrastructure of the United States, and the resource society of Australia may seem unrelated. Yet through these years of observation, they gradually converged into the same question.

Why can some systems continue to evolve, while others gradually fall out of balance?

And this is a question I am still asking today.

I will write this chapter with great restraint.

Because this is not an “author’s autobiography,” but an explanation to readers of why this question has been worth more than twenty years of continued inquiry.

## Chapter 2: What Are We Really Protecting?

Looking back after many years, I realized that all these years I had been doing one thing.

Protecting.

It was just that, at each stage, I thought I was protecting something different.

When I first began working in landscape design, I thought I was protecting beauty. At that time, China was in an era of rapid urbanization, and everyone believed that construction could change the future. We talked about new urbanism, public space, and the harmonious coexistence of people and nature. The 2000 Shanghai World Expo proposed the phrase, “Better City, Better Life,” a sentence later remembered by the world. To this day, I still believe it represented the ideals of many people of that era. We believed that by designing better cities, human beings could gain better lives.

At that time, what I was protecting was the city.

Later, when I worked in Shenzhen, I began to discover that things were not so simple. The differences between the South and the North were far greater than I had imagined. Plants were different, climate was different, space was different, and people’s ways of life were different. The same design method might succeed in one place, yet fail completely in another. At the time, we clearly proposed the concept of “north-south differences,” because we discovered that many seemingly advanced experiences could not simply be copied.

That was the first time I realized that what needed protection might not be a particular design method, but the delicate balance between people and the environment.

Later, I returned to Shanghai and began participating in larger-scale municipal projects and work related to the World Expo. The term climate change began to appear frequently in my field of vision. Sea-level rise, extreme weather, and resource constraints — issues that had once existed only in scientific reports — began to become problems that the real world had to face. I did not believe I could solve climate change, but I began to realize that if the future truly was going to change, then someone needed to understand that change.

So I turned my attention to Xinjiang.

At that time, my thinking was simple. If everyone was discussing coastal cities, then I would go and look at the center of the continent. If sea-level rise would affect the coast, then what would happen inland?

After arriving in Xinjiang, I truly understood aridity for the first time.

Not aridity in books, but aridity on the land; not water scarcity in statistics, but the reality of generations of people continuing to live at the edge of wind, sand, saline-alkali soil, and desert. Those regions continuously sent energy, minerals, and resources outward, yet they rarely became the protagonists under the spotlight. What I saw there was not only an ecological problem, nor only a development problem, but a responsibility.

I began to respect those who had lived for a long time in frontier and desert regions. There was a very special spirit in them. That spirit might not be loud, but it could endure for a long time; it might not be dazzling, but it could persist for a long time. Looking back later, what I truly wanted to protect in Xinjiang may not have been the land itself, but the capacity to keep giving for a larger community.

Later, I went to the United States.

The shock that America gave me was not only about technology and prosperity. The greater shock came from civilization itself. For the first time, I observed China and the world from the perspective of the global industrial chain. I saw large quantities of goods produced in China entering the American market, and I also saw how the United States organized the world through finance, technology, and global supply chains.

At that time, I often thought about one question. Why does a country that bears production responsibilities not necessarily gain an equal voice? Why do so many people who work hard to create value find it so difficult to decide the standards by which value is defined?

From then on, I discovered that what I cared about was no longer only ecology and the environment. I began to care about fairness, dignity, and how different civilizations could build a more balanced relationship with one another.

After 2015, I came to Australia.

If China allowed me to see development, and the United States allowed me to see competition, then Australia allowed me to see another state of civilization.

This is a society rich in resources, mature in institutions, and relatively affluent in daily life. Many people no longer struggle for basic survival, but instead think about how to maintain their existing way of life. When I first arrived here, I once envied this stability. But after living here for a longer time, I gradually began to see another side of it.

A system that has been successful for a long time can also develop inertia.

The sense of security brought by resources can turn into conservatism. The stability brought by institutions can turn into slowness. Past success can even become an obstacle to future innovation.

It was then that I began to realize that the greatest challenge for a civilization may not come from poverty, but from success.

What truly led me into deeper reflection was becoming a father.

In the past, I thought more about cities, the environment, and society. Later, I began to think about children.

My children are growing up in Australia. They speak English and Chinese. They receive a Western education, while also inheriting the cultural background of a Chinese family. Through them, I felt the differences between civilizations in such a direct way for the first time.

I do not believe that any one civilization is naturally superior to another. On the contrary, precisely because I have lived for a long time between two cultures, I have increasingly realized that they understand the world in different ways.

Western civilization is good at classification, decomposition, and specialization. Modern science, legal systems, and industrial civilization are all built on this capacity. Chinese culture, by contrast, is more accustomed to seeking relationships — relationships between people, between humanity and nature, and between the past and the future. Many things that seem independent are often understood as interconnected in Chinese thinking.

This difference exists not only in language, but also in education, social organization, and values.

When I began to think about the kind of world my children would live in, I found that what I cared about was no longer only land, cities, and climate. I began to ask what is truly worth passing on to the next generation.

Is it knowledge?

Is it wealth?

Is it technology?

Or is it a way of understanding the world?

Over these years, I have increasingly felt that the real problem humanity faces is not a shortage of resources, but uncertainty about direction.

China hopes to continue developing. The United States hopes to maintain its lead. Europe hopes to preserve rules. Australia hopes to maintain its way of life. The Middle East still hesitates between war and peace. Different countries, different civilizations, and different groups are all pursuing what they understand to be a better life.

The problem is that these wishes are not always compatible with one another.

In a certain sense, the development of civilization is a process in which different wishes continuously collide, coordinate, and reorganize themselves.

So I began to rethink climate governance.

Many people discuss carbon emissions, energy transition, carbon footprints, and green development. These are all important. But I increasingly feel that carbon emissions are only an indicator, not the final answer.

If one day humanity achieves zero carbon emissions, will our problems be over?

Not necessarily.

Because even if the carbon problem is solved, we will still need to face questions of fairness, civilization, intergenerational responsibility, and the long-term relationship between humanity and nature.

What climate governance ultimately discusses is not carbon.

It is civilization.

And what civilization ultimately discusses is not the speed of development.

It is direction.

Over these years, from Shenzhen to Xinjiang, from the United States to Australia, I always thought I was searching for different answers. Only when I look back today do I realize that what I was truly searching for was the same question all along.

What are we really protecting?

When I was young, I thought protection was an ideal.

Later, I thought protection was a responsibility.

Later still, I thought protection was a civilizational duty.

Today, I increasingly feel that the true meaning of protection may be to leave more possibilities for the future.

Because whether for an individual, a nation, or human civilization as a whole, the most precious thing has never been the answers already possessed.

It is the ability to continue searching for answers.

And it was here that I encountered a new question.

When more and more factors begin to influence one another, and when environment, economy, energy, technology, politics, and culture become increasingly intertwined, can humanity still understand the future in the same way as before?

Or, to put it another way, why is it becoming increasingly difficult for us to predict the future?

This question became the next thread of inquiry that I would continue to follow.

## Chapter 3: Why Is It Becoming Increasingly Difficult to Predict the Future?

When we were children, we actually believed in the future.

People of that era probably still remember that the future meant having upstairs and downstairs, electric lights and telephones. It meant color televisions, refrigerators, and washing machines. It meant that every household could live a better life. At that time, the future was distant, but it was very clear. We believed that as long as we worked hard to develop, the future would certainly become better and better.

Later, these goals were achieved one by one.

We gained cars, mobile phones, the internet, high-speed rail, satellite navigation, and artificial intelligence. Today, the computing power of a phone in the hands of an ordinary person far exceeds the capability of the entire control system used when humanity landed on the moon.

In theory, we should be closer to the future than any previous era.

But strangely, we have become more confused instead.

In recent years, a term has become popular on the internet: the “Mandela Effect.” Many people find that they clearly remember something from childhood in one way, only to later discover that reality seems to have become something else. Some say it is a memory error, some say it is a parallel universe, and others treat it as a joke.

I am not particularly interested in these explanations.

What truly interests me is something else.

Why are more and more people beginning to doubt their own memories?

Why are more and more people beginning to doubt things they once firmly believed?

Perhaps the problem is not that our memories have gone wrong.

Perhaps this era is simply changing too quickly.

So quickly that our brains no longer have time to establish stable reference points.

My generation is probably one of the generations that has personally experienced this most intense kind of change.

When we were young, we believed in the planned economy.

Later, we believed in the market economy.

Later, we believed in globalization.

Later, we believed that the internet would connect the world.

Later, we believed that free trade would make the world more and more open.

Later, we believed that technology could solve more and more problems.

Then suddenly, one day, we discovered that Britain had left the European Union, that the United States had begun to emphasize “America First,” that global supply chains had begun to restructure, that tariff wars had reappeared, and that geopolitics had returned to the center of the stage.

Many people realized for the first time that history is not a straight road moving forward.

The page we thought had already been turned may return again.

The things we thought had already become consensus may once again be questioned.

This feeling does not exist only in China.

It is happening all over the world.

For the past several decades, humanity has believed that globalization would continue to deepen. Capital flowed around the world, industries were deployed globally, knowledge spread across borders, and the internet connected the whole world into one system. Many people believed that humanity would eventually solve common problems through cooperation.

Yet reality did not fully follow the script.

The United Nations still exists, but war has not disappeared.

The IPCC continues to publish reports, but climate change is still intensifying.

The world possesses unprecedented technological capability, yet it still cannot prevent many regions from falling into conflict and turmoil.

So people have begun to ask again:

Did we misunderstand the world in the past, or was the world always more complex than we imagined?

It was in the midst of this confusion that artificial intelligence appeared.

At first, people treated it as a more advanced computing tool.

Later, they discovered that it could not only calculate, but also write, draw, translate, reason, and even accompany human beings in thinking.

This is a very remarkable thing.

For the past several hundred years, humanity has always believed that the greatest characteristic of machines is certainty.

One plus one equals two.

Whatever the input is, the output follows.

The logic is rigorous, and the rules are clear.

Yet the new generation of artificial intelligence appearing today has begun to show a certain capacity for association, imagination, and even creation.

Sometimes it makes mistakes.

Sometimes it talks nonsense.

Sometimes it even makes people feel as if it were a real thinker.

And so a new question emerges.

If machines also begin to possess a certain kind of uncertainty, then how should human beings judge?

In the past, the problem was that there was not enough information.

Today, the problem has become that there is too much information.

In the past, it was difficult for us to find answers.

Today, we receive thousands upon thousands of answers every day.

In the past, people said that hearing is unreliable, but seeing is believing.

Today, videos can be generated, voices can be cloned, and photographs can be forged.

Even the memories we consider most reliable are constantly being challenged.

Humanity seems to be entering such an era for the first time:

Facts are no longer scarce.

Judgment is becoming scarce.

Facing this change, I once also tried to find some kind of definite answer.

Later, I slowly discovered that perhaps the answer itself does not exist.

Or, even if it does exist, it will continue to change with the times.

When we were children, modernization meant having upstairs and downstairs, electric lights and telephones.

Later, modernization meant cars and the internet.

Later still, modernization meant artificial intelligence and quantum computing.

In the future, there may be new definitions again.

Answers keep changing.

What truly matters, perhaps, is not the answer.

It is how we continue to move closer to reality.

Over these years, whether in China, the United States, or Australia, whether in cities, deserts, or forests, I have become increasingly accustomed to using the most clumsy method.

The method of elimination.

Peeling away complicated information layer by layer.

Peeling away emotions.

Peeling away positions.

Peeling away interests.

Then returning to the most original questions.

What does life need?

Why does civilization exist?

Why does the environment matter?

Why should human beings cooperate?

It is like tracing a great river upstream, all the way back to the first snowmelt deep in the Himalayas.

Because only there can we see those things that are most basic, most real, and least easily changed by the times.

Later, I gradually realized that what humanity truly needs may not be a system that can predict the future.

Rather, it may be a framework that can help us continuously observe, continuously validate, and continuously learn.

In recent years, I have temporarily called this idea ClimateOS.

But I do not believe that ClimateOS is the answer.

On the contrary.

One day, it too will be replaced.

Just as the abacus was replaced, just as the steam engine was replaced, and just as the internet will one day be replaced by new tools.

Tools have never been the point.

The point is the problem that those tools are trying to solve.

The meaning of ClimateOS is not to create a new piece of software, nor to create a new platform.

It simply hopes to help humanity preserve one capacity.

The capacity to continuously observe the world, continuously validate reality, and continuously revise our own cognition.

Perhaps one day in the future, humanity really will leave Earth.

Perhaps one day, we will enter the interstellar age.

Perhaps one day, artificial intelligence will become countless times more powerful than it is today.

But no matter where the future leads, we must not forget that we are first and foremost a civilization that came from Earth.

Before entering the interstellar age, we should at least learn how to understand our own home.

Because civilization can continue not because it possesses the correct answer.

It continues because it always preserves the ability to keep searching for answers.

## Chapter 4: ClimateOS: From Predicting the Future to Deepening Our Capacity to Understand It

In the preceding chapters, I have been speaking about imbalance, protection, and the uncertainty of the future.

Many friends, after reading this far, may arrive at a certain feeling: the world is becoming more and more complex, the future is becoming harder and harder to predict, and humanity seems to be losing its ability to understand what lies ahead.

Yet the observations I have gathered over these years have led me to a conclusion that is, in some ways, almost the opposite.

Humanity is not becoming worse at predicting the future.

On the contrary, we may be living in the age with the strongest predictive capacity in human history.

From weather forecasts to population projections, from economic models to climate models, from corporate strategic simulations to national development planning, humanity has built ever larger systems of prediction. Many predictions may not be entirely accurate, but they are by no means castles in the air. Many of the things happening today were, in fact, warned about ten years ago, twenty years ago, or even earlier.

This is true of population aging. It is true of resource constraints. It is true of the energy transition. It is also true of climate change.

The problem has never simply been that we could not predict.

The problem is that even when something has been predicted, it still happens.

Over these years, I have kept thinking about one question. If the United Nations can project population change, if scientists can project climate change, if economists can project cycles of risk, then why does humanity still walk, again and again, into difficulties it has already been warned about?

Later, I gradually came to realize that perhaps the problem has never truly been prediction itself.

Prediction is more like a map.

The real challenge facing civilization is how to act along that map.

Knowing there is a cliff ahead does not mean everyone will stop walking. Knowing that the environment is deteriorating does not mean everyone will change their behavior. Knowing that the future contains risk does not mean the real world will automatically turn toward another path. Between knowledge and action, there has always been an enormous gulf.

Sometimes that gulf comes from interests. Sometimes it comes from institutions. Sometimes it comes from cultural inertia. And sometimes it comes merely from the hesitation and wavering of human nature.

I have come to feel more and more that the root of many major human problems is not a lack of knowledge, but a lack of feedback.

Why does a person make the same mistake again and again? Because the mistake has not become feedback in time. Why does a company move toward decline? Because the market has already changed, while the organization remains immersed in the experience of past success. Why does a country fall into difficulty? Because problems have already appeared, yet the system lacks an effective enough mechanism to complete its own adjustment.

Ecosystems are the same.

I have seen saline-alkali land expanding in Xinjiang. I have seen how arid regions in the United States rely on infrastructure to sustain development. I have seen bushfires and floods take turns in Australia. The more I observed, the more I realized that nature has, in fact, been sending feedback to humanity all along.

A river changing course is feedback.

Falling groundwater levels are feedback.

Forest degradation is feedback.

The decline of species is feedback.

Climate abnormality is feedback as well.

The problem is not that nature has not spoken.

The problem is whether humanity is willing to listen.

For a long time, we were accustomed to treating environmental issues as professional issues. Scientists were responsible for research, governments for decision-making, enterprises for execution, and ordinary people for accepting the results.

This division of labor was reasonable in the industrial age.

At that time, information was limited, computing power was limited, and the capacity to participate was also limited.

But today, the situation has changed.

Artificial intelligence is gradually opening up analytical capabilities that once belonged to a small number of people. A student, a farmer, an engineer, or a community volunteer may all now have the opportunity to access data, knowledge, and analytical tools that were once difficult to reach.

For the first time in human history, large-scale cognitive capability is beginning to spread into society.

Yet at the same time, we have encountered a new problem.

There is more and more information, but judgment is becoming harder.

There are more and more models, but less and less consensus.

There are richer and richer answers, yet action is not necessarily becoming more effective.

In the past, the problem was that we could not see the future.

Today, the problem is that there are too many versions of the future.

Everyone has their own explanation. Everyone has their own logic. Everyone has their own answer. Vast amounts of information collide with one another, and countless viewpoints compete with one another, yet mechanisms capable of sustained learning and sustained correction remain scarce.

It was precisely in this process that an idea gradually began to take shape in my mind.

Perhaps the most important thing in the future will not be who possesses the most powerful predictive model.

It will be who can keep learning.

Who can keep validating.

Who can keep correcting their understanding of the world.

System imbalance itself is not terrifying.

What is truly terrifying is that imbalance has already begun, while the system has lost the ability to detect it.

The uncertainty of the future is not terrifying either.

What is truly terrifying is that, in the face of uncertainty, humanity loses its capacity to learn.

If the first three industrial revolutions changed humanity’s ability to use resources, then the intelligent age may truly change humanity’s ability to understand itself.

And so I began to rethink one question.

If the future cannot be predicted with perfect accuracy, can we at least keep improving our ability to understand it?

If the world keeps changing, can we establish a mechanism for continuous learning?

If every prediction will eventually contain error, can we give the system the ability to continuously correct those errors?

Later, I temporarily gave this idea the name ClimateOS.

But I have always believed that ClimateOS is not the answer.

It may not even be the future.

One day, like many advanced technologies today, it will be replaced by new technologies and updated by new systems.

What truly matters has never been the tool itself.

It is the capacity carried within the tool.

What ClimateOS hopes to address has never been how to predict the future with perfect accuracy.

Because the future is, by its nature, full of change.

The problem ClimateOS hopes to address is how to keep learning amid change, how to keep correcting through learning, and how, through that correction, to gradually deepen humanity’s ability to understand the future.

Prediction matters.

But validation matters more.

Answers matter.

But learning why an answer is right, and why it is wrong, matters even more.

Because civilization endures not because it possesses the one correct answer.

It endures because it preserves the ability to keep moving closer to reality.

And I believe this may be the direction most worth exploring for future environmental governance, climate governance, and even the governance of civilization itself.

## Chapter 5: Why Validation Matters More Than Prediction

Over the years, as I have participated in and observed environmental governance, climate governance, and regional development, I have come to feel more and more that humanity does not really lack goals.

Whether it is China’s “dual-carbon” strategy or the ASRS framework now being advanced in Australia; whether it is the European Union’s steadily evolving carbon border mechanism or the global emissions-reduction framework promoted through successive United Nations climate conferences, all of these efforts are trying to answer the same question: how can humanity continue to develop while reducing, as much as possible, the debt we pass on to the future?

On the surface, this may seem to be simply a question of emissions reduction. But as I have come into contact with the development practices of different regions, different industries, and different countries, I have gradually realized that it is, in fact, answering a deeper question: after humanity has gained increasingly powerful productive capacity, how should we live in relation to the future?

The difficulty is that once these goals enter the real world, things often become far more complicated than they first appear.

The eastern coastal regions and the western energy bases do not see the same world. Emerging industries and traditional industries do not face the same pressures. Resource-exporting regions and resource-consuming regions do not carry the same responsibilities. A region that shoulders the task of supplying energy for an entire nation may naturally have higher carbon emissions. A region that consumes large amounts of energy may also generate greater economic output. In many cases, the issue is not simply who is right and who is wrong. It is that everyone is looking at the problem from the place where they stand.

In recent years, as China has advanced its dual-carbon strategy, similar discussions have already begun to emerge. How should old industrial bases transform? How should resource-based regions bear responsibility for emissions reduction? How should the new energy sector develop in coordination with traditional industries? These questions do not have standard answers. Australia faces similar challenges, and so does Europe. The European Union is building its own carbon standards system, and China is also building its own. Yet their understandings of carbon value, carbon accounting, and industrial contribution often differ.

People are often inclined to attribute these problems to policy design or to the level of implementation. But the more I observe, the more I feel that the deeper reason may be that we are entering a governance environment humanity has never truly faced before.

Most problems in the industrial age were local problems.

Today’s problems are increasingly systemic.

In the past, building a road meant solving the problem of a road. Building a bridge meant solving the problem of a bridge. Developing an industrial park meant solving the development problem of a particular area. Today, however, an energy project may affect the environment, industry, finance, employment, and international trade at the same time. A climate policy may simultaneously affect corporate costs, local fiscal capacity, social fairness, and global competition.

The problems are becoming more complex, and the number of participants is also increasing.

Government is one part. Enterprises are one part. Capital is one part. Research institutions are one part. The general public is also one part. In the future, as artificial intelligence and agent systems become involved on a large scale, the number of actors capable of shaping future development will grow even further.

It was then that I began to realize that humanity’s greatest future challenge may not be to find one answer that everyone agrees with, but to allow different people to see where one another stand.

Only when people can see where others stand can they understand the responsibilities others carry. Only when they understand those responsibilities can real coordination become possible. And this kind of coordination is precisely what many governance systems lack most.

Over the years, I have often relied on a rather clumsy way of thinking.

The method of elimination.

I keep tracing complex problems upward, layer by layer, searching for the questions that determine other questions.

The economy matters, but the economy depends on energy. Energy matters, but energy depends on resources. Resources matter, but resources depend on ecosystems. And ecosystems, in the end, are shaped by the long-term interaction of climate, water, land, and human activity.

Slowly, I began to see that many problems that appear entirely different eventually converge in the same place.

If human civilization as a whole is imagined as a tree, then finance, industry, trade, technology, population, and education are more like its branches and leaves. Climate, water resources, land, energy, and ecosystems are closer to the roots that support the growth of the entire tree.

The leaves matter, of course. The branches matter as well. But if something goes wrong with the roots, even the most luxuriant crown cannot endure for long.

It is for this reason that I began to understand validation in a new way.

For a long time, humanity has placed greater emphasis on prediction.

Predicting future population.
Predicting economic growth.
Predicting climate change.
Predicting industrial trends.

All of this work is extremely important, and it has indeed helped humanity build a capacity to understand the future.

But over these years, I have increasingly come to see that prediction is only the beginning.

The truly difficult part is validation.

Prediction tells us what may happen, while validation tells us what has actually happened. Prediction helps us establish a direction, while validation helps us correct that direction. Prediction is the ability to imagine the future; validation is the ability to learn from the future.

Human civilization has not continued to move forward because every prediction was correct. It has moved forward because every error, sooner or later, could be discovered, corrected, absorbed, and transformed into new experience.

This is true for individuals. It is true for enterprises. It is true for nations. It is true for civilization itself.

I gradually came to realize that the truly important part of ClimateOS may not be prediction.

It may not even be artificial intelligence.

Prediction models will continue to be updated. Artificial intelligence will continue to iterate. The most advanced technology of today may be replaced tomorrow by something new.

What is truly worth preserving may be a capacity.

A capacity that allows different actors to see where they stand in relation to one another. A capacity that allows action to produce feedback. A capacity that allows feedback to become learning. A capacity that allows learning to settle into long-term memory.

When many people first hear about ClimateOS, they imagine it as a vast system.

In fact, I increasingly do not see it that way.

If one day it truly has value, then it should ultimately become smaller and smaller.

Small enough to exist like air. Small enough to exist like water. Small enough to become a basic language embedded within different AIs, different industrial systems, and different governance systems.

People may not necessarily remember its name, just as today few people remember the names of the underlying protocols of the internet. But the capacity it carries — the shared ability to understand the environment, to understand one another, and to understand the future — may continue to exist.

In this sense, ClimateOS is not an answer.

It is more like a way of continually approaching the answer.

It is not a destination, but a process of continuous learning.

Because what allows a civilization to endure has never been the possession of some permanently correct conclusion. It is the ability to keep revising itself.

And validation is the most important starting point of that ability.

## Chapter 6: From Tree-like Organizations to a Neural-Network Civilization

By this point in the discussion, the question has already moved from “how to predict the future” to “how to understand the future.” If prediction asks what may happen, then validation asks whether what we are doing is actually working. And once validation begins to occur again and again, a deeper question inevitably arises: how can these forms of feedback be truly understood by different people, different organizations, and different regions — and then be transformed into new action?

For a long time, human society has been accustomed to organizing the world through tree-like structures. Governments have hierarchies. Companies have departments. Schools have faculties. Armies have command systems. Cities have centers, districts, and boundaries. This structure is not wrong. On the contrary, it has been highly effective for much of human history, because it enables division of labor, management, and the basic order required by complex societies.

But tree-like structures also have an obvious limitation. They are good at expressing vertical relationships, but poor at expressing horizontal relationships. They are good at defining responsibilities and boundaries, but poor at expressing dynamic feedback. When a problem belongs to one department, one industry, or one region, a tree-like structure can handle it well. But when a problem simultaneously involves energy, land, climate, industry, finance, employment, and international rules, the traditional tree begins to feel heavy and slow.

This is where the difficulty of many governance problems today truly begins. Dual-carbon transition is not simply an energy issue. ESG is not simply a corporate issue. Carbon footprint is not simply a matter of technical accounting. One end of these questions connects to national strategy, while the other connects to corporate costs. One end connects to international trade, while the other connects to the everyday choices of ordinary consumers. Each actor sees only the position from which they stand, but few can see how the whole system is moving.

When the whole cannot be seen, rules easily become pressure. Companies feel that they are merely being regulated. Local governments feel that they are merely being assessed. Individuals feel that these matters are far removed from their own lives. Over time, governance becomes an externally imposed task rather than an internally awakened action. Without understanding, there is little inner drive. Without inner drive, even the best goals may turn into resistance during implementation.

This is why I increasingly believe that what future governance truly needs to build is not only a system of rules, but a system of perception. Every region needs to know where it stands within the nation’s ecological and industrial structure. Every industry needs to know where it stands within the energy and carbon-emissions system. Every enterprise needs to know where it stands within the supply chain and within environmental responsibility. Only when one knows where one is can one understand why change is necessary, and only then can one understand where one’s contribution truly lies.

This makes me think of trees, and also of nerves.

A tree has roots, trunk, branches, and leaves. Its structure is clear, and its layers are distinct. Many of the organizational systems human beings built in the past were like trees. But life does not exist through structure alone. Animals have nervous systems. Human beings have nervous systems. Even within plants, there are forms of signal transmission and feedback. True vitality is not merely the growth of branches and leaves, but the ability to sense change, transmit signals, and adjust oneself.

In other words, structure determines whether a system can stand. Feedback determines whether a system is alive.

Industrial-age human society has already built extremely complex structures. Nations, cities, corporations, markets, laws, schools, and research institutions are all the skeleton of modern civilization. But the problem today is that while the skeleton grows larger and larger, the nerves are not necessarily sensitive enough. Many problems have already emerged, and many signals have already appeared. Yet the system transmits them too slowly, understands them too slowly, and corrects itself too slowly.

The internet once tried to solve the problem of connection. It made information flow faster, made communication easier, and, for the first time, brought the world into an age of real-time transmission. But the internet solved the connection of information; it did not necessarily solve the connection of cognition. Information can spread rapidly, but so can misunderstanding. Opinions can spread rapidly, but so can conflict. Connection by itself does not automatically produce understanding.

The age of AI brings a new possibility. Artificial intelligence can not only transmit information; it can also help human beings analyze relationships, identify patterns, and explain change. If the internet allowed human society to possess a denser information network for the first time, then AI may allow that network to gradually acquire a certain cognitive capacity. It may not only tell us what has happened, but also help us understand why it happened, who is affected, and how the next adjustment should be made.

But this also brings new risks. Every AI, every Agent, and every organizational system may continue optimizing around its own task. If there is no higher-level shared reference, they may become more and more efficient, but not necessarily more and more correct. An enterprise Agent may optimize profit to the extreme. ## 10.2 They Are Different Systems, but the Same Dynamics

Climate science often introduces the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC), the Walker Circulation, the Hadley Cell, subtropical high-pressure systems, monsoons, El Niño and La Niña as separate topics. Textbooks naturally divide them into different chapters because each possesses its own characteristics. However, after comparing drylands across continents, I gradually realized that they are not separate systems at all. They are different manifestations of the same planetary dynamics observed from different spatial scales and under different boundary conditions.

What changes is not the underlying physics but the environment in which that physics operates. Ocean currents emerge because seawater differs in temperature and salinity. Atmospheric circulation emerges because air differs in temperature and pressure. Wind interacts with mountains, forests and cities, continuously reshaping local weather. Although these systems appear independent, they all follow the same sequence: energy creates differences, differences generate gradients, gradients drive movement, movement redistributes energy, and the redistribution of energy establishes a new set of gradients.

From the perspective of ClimateOS, the objective is therefore not to predict the duration of a particular El Niño event or the exact arrival of a monsoon. The objective is to recognize the common dynamics shared by all these phenomena. ## 10.3 The Earth Does Not Seek Stability, It Seeks Dynamic Balance

When we hear the word "balance," we often imagine stillness: a calm lake, a motionless rock, or an unchanging landscape. The Earth, however, has never been static. Oceans circulate continuously. The atmosphere moves relentlessly. Forests grow and decay. Ice sheets advance and retreat. Human societies transform landscapes at unprecedented speed. If stability meant the absence of movement, the Earth has never been stable.

What actually governs the Earth is **dynamic equilibrium**. Every day the Sun delivers enormous amounts of energy to our planet, while the Earth continuously radiates energy back into space. The equator receives more solar energy than the poles. Oceans store heat differently from continents. Water evaporates, condenses, precipitates and flows back to the sea. Every movement exists not to eliminate motion but to reduce differences within the system.

This fundamental principle has long been described by physics as the conservation of energy. Energy cannot be created or destroyed; it can only be transformed and redistributed. Wind, ocean circulation, vegetation growth, the carbon cycle and even human energy consumption all participate in this continuous redistribution process. Climate change is therefore not simply a collection of weather events. It represents a long-term adjustment in the Earth's energy balance.

Ultimately, ClimateOS is not designed to predict individual storms. It is designed to identify the forces that gradually alter dynamic equilibrium. A storm eventually ends. A flood eventually recedes. Even El Niño eventually weakens. But if the underlying energy gradients continue to evolve, new events will inevitably emerge.

## 10.4 Mass Is Conserved

If energy is continuously redistributed, another equally fundamental question arises: what exactly is being moved? Air does not appear from nowhere. Water does not disappear without trace. Carbon, nutrients, sediments and moisture are constantly transferred between oceans, atmosphere, land and living organisms. They change form and location, but they remain subject to one of the most fundamental principles of nature: **mass is conserved.**

The continuity equation expresses this simple but profound idea.

$$
\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}+\nabla\cdot(\rho\mathbf{u})=0
$$

Although the equation appears abstract, its meaning is remarkably intuitive. Any increase in mass within a region must be explained by inflow exceeding outflow. Likewise, any decrease must result from more material leaving than entering. Wind, rivers, ocean currents and atmospheric circulation all obey this same relationship.

For ClimateOS, the significance of the continuity equation extends beyond mathematics. It reminds us that no environmental phenomenon exists in isolation. A drought in one region often corresponds to excessive rainfall elsewhere. Carbon stored in forests must originate from the atmosphere. Ocean circulation weakens not because water disappears, but because the pathways of mass transport change. Every local observation belongs to a larger planetary continuity.

## 10.5 The Earth Moves Because Differences Exist

If continuity explains why matter is conserved, another question naturally follows: why does anything move at all?

The answer lies in one word: **difference**.

The Earth is fundamentally heterogeneous. Solar radiation is unevenly distributed between the equator and the poles. Land heats faster than oceans. Mountains differ from plains. Temperature, pressure, humidity and salinity vary continuously across space. These differences create gradients, and gradients generate movement.

The Earth therefore behaves as an open system that continuously reduces imbalance rather than seeking complete uniformity. Heat flows from warmer regions to cooler ones. Air moves from higher pressure to lower pressure. Water flows downhill under gravity. Ocean currents redistribute density differences. Every circulation is an expression of gradients attempting to reorganize the Earth's energy and material distribution.

CCZPS emerged from this realization. Rather than classifying climates, it seeks to organize evolving gradient fields. Temperature, humidity, wind, ocean circulation, vegetation and human activity become interconnected components of one continuously changing system.

## Chapter 11: The Meridians of the Earth: From Climate Dynamics to Civilizational Regulation

By this point, CCZPS is no longer merely a planning system, nor is it simply a tool for climate analysis. What it must truly face is a more fundamental question: how does the Earth system itself operate, and where, within this system, can humanity find its proper position of action?

If we imagine the Earth as a living body, then atmospheric circulation, ocean circulation, the water cycle, groundwater movement, and heat transfer are like its meridians. They are not always directly visible to human eyes, yet over long periods of time they determine the heat and cold, wetness and dryness, wind direction, rainfall, vegetation, and distribution of civilizations across the surface of the Earth. The droughts, floods, heat waves, cold surges, and bushfires we usually see are often only surface symptoms of changes taking place within these deeper flows.

The so-called Hadley circulation can be understood as a vast atmospheric cycle in which heated air rises near the equator, moves outward at high altitude toward both sides, and then descends in the subtropics. It helps explain why many deserts appear around twenty to thirty degrees north and south latitude. The Ferrel circulation lies in the mid-latitudes and forms part of the complex energy exchange between the tropics and the polar regions. The Walker circulation mainly occurs along the east-west direction of the equatorial Pacific, and is closely related to El Niño and La Niña, influencing rainfall patterns and climate anomalies around the world.

These concepts may appear to belong to meteorology, but they are not merely meteorological concepts. They determine where farmland can remain stable, where cities can expand, where water resources can accumulate, and where energy demand will rise. In other words, they are not a background. They are part of the underlying forces that shape the development of civilization.

The AMOC — the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation — is like a gigantic heat-transport system within the ocean. It carries heat from lower latitudes toward higher latitudes, helping maintain the climatic balance of the North Atlantic and surrounding regions. If this system weakens or behaves abnormally, the consequences may affect not only European weather, but also agriculture, energy, shipping, population migration, and geopolitics.

So what we are discussing is not mysticism, but something built upon foundations that modern science has already shown to be valid. For example, the classical continuity equation in fluid dynamics is:

$$
\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{u}) = 0
$$

In plain human language, this means that mass does not appear out of nowhere, nor does it disappear into nothing. It moves.

And this is precisely what the previous discussion has been pointing toward:

Wind flows. Water flows. Heat flows. Resources flow. Populations flow. Civilization, too, flows.

We may then introduce the principle of energy conservation:

$$
\Delta E = Q - W
$$

The change of any system is, in essence, the result of energy entering and leaving that system.

This is true of ecosystems. It is true of cities. It is true of nations. It is true of civilizations.

Therefore, what CCZPS truly cares about is not a single event, but dynamic relationships. A flood, a drought, or a heat wave, taken by itself, cannot explain the whole problem. The key question is whether these events arise from deeper systemic changes. If they do, then we must ask: along what pathways will such changes continue to transmit, and in which regions, industries, and groups of people will their consequences appear?

Here, a new way of understanding becomes necessary. In the past, we were accustomed to managing the world according to administrative boundaries drawn on the surface of the land. But the Earth system does not operate according to administrative boundaries. Wind does not stop at a national border. Water does not flow according to provincial lines. Heat does not obey an urban planning map. Human management boundaries are artificial, while the dynamic boundaries of nature arise from flow itself.

If traditional planning is more like arranging land on a map, then CCZPS is more like identifying key nodes within flows. What it seeks are the “acupoints” capable of influencing the direction of a system. This is somewhat like the meridians and acupuncture of traditional Chinese medicine, and also like the Chinese martial idea of using four ounces of force to move a thousand pounds. Truly skillful regulation is not about using enormous force to forcibly change everything. It is about understanding the operating patterns of a system, and then finding the positions where small interventions may produce large effects.

Climate governance is the same. Humanity has already affected the Earth system through carbon emissions, land development, energy consumption, and urban expansion. But we have not yet truly learned how to understand our own influence in a systemic way. We know about warming. We know about emissions reduction. We know about green electricity. We know about carbon accounting. Yet we still do not fully understand how these actions form long-term feedback with the atmosphere, the ocean, land, groundwater, and ecosystems.

This is the core point that Chapter 11 seeks to establish: the Earth is not a static map, but a dynamic living system. CCZPS does not seek to replace meteorological models, ocean models, or economic models. Rather, it seeks to establish dynamic connections among these models. It cares about how the surface, the underground, the sky, the ocean, and human activity transmit influence to one another, form feedback, and alter the possibilities of the future.

For this reason, the scientific foundation of CCZPS is not absolute certainty, but probabilistic dynamics. It acknowledges uncertainty. And precisely because it acknowledges uncertainty, it requires continuous observation, continuous validation, and continuous correction. As satellite remote sensing, ground sensors, AI models, digital twins, and computing power continue to improve, uncertainty will not disappear, but it can be gradually compressed, and the precision of understanding can continue to increase.

In this sense, what CCZPS truly wants to do is guide humanity from “seeing phenomena” toward “understanding meridians.” Seeing phenomena allows only emergency response. Understanding meridians makes regulation possible. Seeing floods, droughts, heat waves, and bushfires is only the first step. Understanding the flows, feedbacks, and dynamic structures behind them is the next step that future climate governance and civilizational governance must take.

If ClimateOS provides an environmental cognitive operating setting, then CCZPS provides the capacity for dynamic identification. The former helps us build shared cognition; the latter helps us find key nodes. One answers the question, “How do we see the world together?” The other answers the question, “How do we understand the way the world is flowing?”

This also opens a direction for the next stage of work. In the future, we need to place atmospheric circulation, ocean circulation, the water cycle, underground resource flows, energy flows, and human social activity into the same dynamic map for observation. Only in this way can we truly understand the meridians of the Earth system. And only in this way can humanity find its position for continuing forward without damaging the vitality of the whole.

## Chapter 12: Why Civilization Is Worth Continuing

By this point, I have become increasingly unwilling to discuss one question: what exactly is humanity?

Yet I also saw another problem: when a society remains comfortable for a long time, it may gradually lose the impulse to continue exploring forward.

I once approached people of different religions with this question in mind: Buddhism, Daoism, Christianity, Islam, and even the traditional beliefs of Aboriginal Australians, in which all things possess spirit. Some entrust the answer to God.

Some entrust the answer to nature.

Some entrust the answer to the afterlife.

Some entrust the answer to faith.

Although their forms of expression are different, they all strive to help human beings establish a meaning that reaches beyond the length of an individual life.

Machines can calculate.

AI can learn.

In the future, it may even become far more intelligent than we are.

Because civilization is worth continuing not because it is powerful enough.

It is worth continuing because it is still willing to become better.

## Chapter 13: Validating Civilization
— Why Validation Matters More Than Prediction

In recent years, people have often discussed predicting the future. Economists predict markets, scientists predict climate change, governments predict demographic structures, and enterprises predict industrial trends. It seems that whoever can predict the future more accurately will hold the initiative. However, when I look back at the development of the past few decades, I gradually come to a question: has human history really lacked prediction?

The answer is clearly no.

On almost every major issue, including population aging, resource constraints, climate change, ecological degradation, and financial risk, warnings have been raised in advance. The United Nations has made predictions, scientists have made predictions, strategic research institutions have made predictions, and even before the decline of many civilizations, there were people who could already see the problems.

Then the question arises. If predictions have always existed, why do mistakes still keep repeating? Why do crises still keep happening? Why do some civilizations still eventually decline?

I later gradually realized that the problem often does not lie in prediction, but in validation.

Chinese people are actually not unfamiliar with this logic. We do not necessarily use the word Validation, but we have always believed in cause and effect. When farmers plant crops, they look at the harvest. When craftsmen make objects, they look at quality. When doctors prescribe medicine, they look at the effect. When a dynasty governs the world, it looks at the hearts of the people. These seemingly different things are essentially doing the same thing: validation.

In other words, humanity has never lacked the ability to judge the future. What is truly scarce is the ability to test whether we are still walking on the right road.

In the past, people relied on experience to understand cause and effect. The rise and fall of a village, the inheritance of a family, and the improvement of a craft often occurred within limited space and limited time scales. When mistakes appeared, they could be seen. When direction deviated, it could be corrected in time. But after entering industrial civilization, things began to become completely different.

An energy policy may affect decades. An urban plan may change the lives of several generations. An industrial transformation may affect hundreds of millions of people. A global climate governance action may even change the way the entire Earth system operates. Cause and effect still exist, but the causal chain has become longer, feedback has become slower, and the number of participating actors has increased.

At this point, relying only on experience is no longer enough.

Can reducing carbon emissions definitely solve climate change? Is developing new energy definitely more sustainable than traditional energy? Can afforestation definitely restore ecosystems? Will the development of artificial intelligence definitely bring a fairer society?

These questions do not have simple answers. Any action may produce completely different results across different time scales, spatial scales, and social scales. Many times, we think we have solved one problem, but we may have created a new problem somewhere else.

This is why I later paid more and more attention to Validation, rather than simply focusing on Prediction.

Prediction focuses on what may happen in the future. Validation focuses on whether we are still moving in the right direction. Prediction is a form of judgment. Validation is a form of testing. Prediction faces the unknown. Validation faces ourselves.

From this perspective, Validation is not a technical term, but a civilizational capacity.

Whether a civilization is advanced does not depend on whether it never makes mistakes, but on whether it retains the ability to admit mistakes, correct mistakes, and relearn. In history, many civilizations were not defeated by external enemies. Rather, after mistakes accumulated, they gradually lost the ability to correct themselves. They still possessed wealth, power, and technology, but they could no longer re-examine themselves.

I once thought about a question. If one day in the future humanity really leaves Earth and begins interstellar travel, what must we take with us? Energy technology? Artificial intelligence? Advanced materials?

All of these are important, but I increasingly feel that there is something even more important: the ability to continuously validate our own behavior.

Because wherever humanity goes, it will face new environments, new resource conditions, and new social structures. If we persist in a wrong judgment for too long, even the most advanced technology cannot save the system from collapse. Future interstellar civilization will need not only the ability to predict the future, but even more the ability to constantly validate and correct itself.

It was under this line of thinking that I began to re-understand the meaning of ClimateOS and CCZPS.

They are not intended to prove that one theory is forever correct, nor are they intended to find an answer that will never make mistakes. On the contrary, their meaning lies precisely in helping us continuously observe, continuously validate, continuously learn, and correct our judgments in time when new evidence appears.

In a sense, the Validation Layer is not a software module, but more like the immune system of civilization. It is responsible for discovering deviations, identifying risks, recording feedback, and helping the whole system continuously adjust its own state.

In the past, humanity validated land. Later, humanity validated machines. Today, humanity has begun to validate cities, industries, and policies. In the future, humanity may need to validate the development direction of civilization as a whole.

Therefore, I increasingly believe that the true maturity of a civilization does not come from possessing all the answers, but from learning to continuously test its answers.

Perhaps Validation is not a new concept. It is only a new expression, in the age of complex systems, of the ancient question humanity has pursued for thousands of years: cause and effect.

Prediction determines how far we can see. Validation determines whether we are still walking on the right road. And a civilization can continue not because it never makes mistakes, but because it always preserves the ability to admit mistakes, correct mistakes, and relearn.

## Chapter 14: After Understanding, How Do We Govern Together?

When we discuss prediction, validation, and learning, a new question naturally arises. Over the past several decades, humanity has never stopped trying to govern. From the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change to the Paris Agreement, from the European Union’s Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism to China’s dual-carbon strategy, from corporate ESG to Australia’s ASRS, all kinds of systems, standards, and rules have emerged one after another. Almost every country and every industry is trying to find its own solution. On the surface, humanity seems to possess the largest governance system in history.

And yet, at the same time, people’s anxiety has not diminished.

Climate change still exists, and extreme weather is becoming more frequent. The energy transition continues to move forward, yet it also brings new competition for resources. Global trade is being restructured, and new standards and barriers keep appearing. We possess more data, more models, and more institutions than ever before, yet we do not seem to have gained more certainty because of them.

And so I began to ask: where exactly is the problem?

Over these years, in planning, engineering, and environmental projects, I have gradually noticed a phenomenon that is both interesting and deeply real. Many projects are highly successful at the beginning. The launch meetings are lively, the promotional materials are polished, the consulting reports are thick, and the financing plans appear complete. Everyone talks about the future. Everyone describes a blueprint.

But five years later, or ten years later, when we look back, there are not many projects that can truly continue operating.

The person who proposed the concept has completed their task. The consulting firm has completed its task. The design team has completed its task. The construction team has completed its task. But when the project enters the stage of long-term operation, funding begins to shrink, attention begins to fade, and responsibility becomes heavier and heavier. Many systems do not die from failure in construction. They die from an insufficient capacity for long-term operation. And this is not a problem of one country alone. It is a common problem faced by modern industrial civilization.

Chinese people are not unfamiliar with this. Whether we speak of Dujiangyan, the Grand Canal, or the waterworks of successive dynasties, they all emphasize one thing: a matter is not finished when it is built; it must be carried forward. Chinese culture has long contained a tendency toward long-termism. One generation plants trees so that later generations may enjoy the shade. A new official still has to deal with old accounts. After one Five-Year Plan, another Five-Year Plan follows. It may not always be perfect, but behind it there has always been an idea of continuous construction across generations.

The Western system, by contrast, reveals another kind of strength. It is extremely good at innovation, at transaction, and at concentrating resources over a short period of time to make something happen. Capital markets, legal systems, entrepreneurial mechanisms, and the consulting industry together form an efficient mechanism of initiation. But very often, once the story has been told, the financing has ended, and the project has landed, long-term operation becomes the weakest link.

This is not a simple matter of superiority or inferiority. It is a difference between two civilizational logics of governance.

One side places greater emphasis on continuous construction; the other places greater emphasis on immediate response. One is skilled at long-term organization; the other is skilled at rapid innovation. The problem is that when we face climate change, energy transition, and global governance — problems that cross regions, scales, and generations — any single logic begins to appear insufficient.

In recent years, this tension has become increasingly visible.

The European Union has introduced the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, hoping to use new rules to promote global emissions reduction. China is advancing its dual-carbon strategy, seeking balance between development and emissions reduction. Australia is implementing ASRS, hoping to establish a new corporate disclosure system. Behind every system lies its own rationality, and also its own real pressures. Yet at the same time, more and more enterprises are beginning to feel something else: there are more forms, thicker reports, stricter audits, while the inner motivation to truly improve the environment does not necessarily grow at the same pace.

ESG, which originally began from environmental concerns, has in many settings gradually become a tool of governance and compliance. Environmental goals are converted into indicators. Indicators are converted into reports. Reports are converted again into audit requirements. What many companies care about most is no longer the environment itself, but how to pass the audit, how to satisfy the requirements, and how to avoid penalties.

And so a new problem emerges: the governance system grows larger and larger, while the object of governance becomes more and more distant.

In fact, whether we speak of the European Union’s carbon border mechanism, China’s dual-carbon system, or global ESG practice, they are all trying to solve the same question: how can humanity pursue development while maintaining the stable operation of environmental systems? Yet environmental systems themselves extend across time and space. Reducing emissions in one region may affect the industrial structure of another. The energy policy of one country may reshape the structure of global supply chains. The transformation of one industry may affect the employment and daily lives of several generations.

These relationships have already far exceeded the scale that traditional governance can easily handle.

I once participated in Australian landscape industry forums, and I have also observed the development of many professional organizations. What I found regrettable was that many organizations, when discussing sustainability, resilience, and the future, rarely truly discussed those questions that cross regions, scales, and generations. People find it easier to discuss immediate interests, immediate systems, and immediate responsibilities. Far fewer are willing to discuss the consequences that may appear decades later.

This is not because people do not care about the future. It is because we lack a tool through which we can understand the future together. For this reason, I increasingly believe that the most important task of future governance is not to keep adding new institutions, new forms, or new assessments, but to build a new shared cognitive capacity. And this is exactly the question that ClimateOS attempts to answer.

ClimateOS is not a new management agency, nor is it a new center of power. It is more like a public language — a cognitive framework that allows governments, enterprises, communities, scientists, and ordinary people to see one another’s positions at the same time. In the past, different groups used different languages. Scientists spoke of models. Enterprises spoke of costs. Governments spoke of policies. Investors spoke of risks. The public spoke of everyday life. Everyone was discussing the same world, yet it became harder and harder for them to understand one another.

What ClimateOS tries to build is not a new authority, but a new connection.

It hopes to help different actors understand their own positions, understand the positions of others, and understand the relationships among them. It allows people not only to know what they are doing, but also to understand how those actions will affect the whole system.

When understanding becomes part of governance, governance can begin to move beyond short-term targets and enter long-term coordination. Therefore, what ClimateOS truly seeks to solve is not the environmental problem itself, but the governance problem.

It does not attempt to replace the dual-carbon system. It does not attempt to replace ESG, ASRS, or any other standard. On the contrary, it is more like a connecting layer among these systems, reorganizing information that was once scattered and placing isolated goals back into the same environmental context.

Because true governance has never been control.

True governance is the ability for different people to understand the same future together.

And when this shared understanding begins to take shape, humanity may finally move from environmental governance toward Earth system governance.

## Chapter 15: Possibility Computing
— From Predicting the Future to Computing the Future

Humanity has always wanted to predict the future.

From ancient times, when people observed the heavens and measured the seasons, to today, when we build supercomputers, artificial intelligence, and all kinds of complex models, we have always been trying to answer the same question: what will the future be like?

Yet the more I have thought about this over the years, the more I have come to feel that perhaps the question itself contains a certain limitation. The future has never been singular. If the future had only one answer, then humanity would not truly need planning, nor would it need governance. Everything would unfold along a predetermined track, and the only thing we could do would be to wait for the result to arrive. It is precisely because the future contains countless directions, countless paths, and countless outcomes that we must continue to judge, validate, and act.

This is why I gradually moved from “predicting the future” toward “computing the future.”

Traditional prediction is more like the search for an answer. People always hope to find the most accurate result. They want to know what will happen tomorrow, what will happen ten years from now, and what will happen a century from now. But as my thinking has deepened, I have increasingly realized that what truly matters is not the answer, but the path. The future is not a result that descends from the sky. It is a process formed through the interaction of countless forces.

If prediction searches for the destination, possibility computing pays closer attention to the road.

In fact, humanity has already begun to work in this way. A city does not have only one planning option. A nation does not have only one energy strategy. An enterprise does not place all of its resources on a single choice. People are always comparing different plans, revising different pathways, and seeking a relatively better direction among them. In Western engineering fields, this method is often called a matrix. Chinese people may understand it more naturally as “parallel options, comparative selection.” On the surface, this is a technical method. In essence, it reflects a new way of cognition.

Because the greatest change of the complex-systems age is that humanity has begun to accept that the future is not singular.

In the past, we were accustomed to searching for certainty. Today, we must learn to understand uncertainty. In the past, we hoped to use one model to solve every problem. Today, we have discovered that behind any problem lie interactions among temporal scales, spatial scales, and social scales. This is true of climate change. It is true of the energy transition. It is true of population structure. It is equally true of the development of artificial intelligence. Problems are becoming more complex, variables are multiplying, and the future is therefore revealing a richer and richer field of possibilities.

This understanding did not come from books alone. It came from years of walking, observing, and living with questions.

From Xinjiang to Shanghai, from the United States to Australia, I once thought I was studying different problems. At first, I was concerned with aridity. Later, I became concerned with climate change. Later still, I became concerned with ecology, energy, water resources, and regional development. But as these problems continued to overlap, I gradually realized that they were all pointing in the same direction.

Air flows. Water flows. Heat flows. Population flows. Capital flows. Information flows as well. Phenomena that seem unrelated on the surface often share deeper driving forces beneath them. The urban expansion, industrial migration, ecological restoration, and social transformation that we see are, in essence, the results of these flows acting over long periods of time.

And so I began to realize that the future is not one single result. It is a field of many possibilities formed through the interaction of these forces.

This is also the point from which CCZPS gradually began to take shape.

At the beginning, I simply wanted to understand climate. Later, I wanted to understand ecology. Later still, I wanted to understand the relationships among energy, water resources, and land use. As the inquiry deepened, I discovered that what I was actually pursuing was something more fundamental: where do the forces that drive civilizational change come from, and how do they work together to shape the future?

And so CCZPS began to move beyond being merely a climate zoning planning system.

It gradually evolved into a cognitive framework for understanding complex systems.

What it cares about is not what will definitely happen in the future, but why different futures may happen. It seeks to help people understand what different outcomes may emerge if we take different actions today; how the whole system may change if one key variable is altered; and where a particular development path may eventually lead civilization if it continues over time.

From this perspective, what CCZPS truly computes is not the result, but possibility.

It is concerned not with the answer, but with the path; not with the destination, but with the process; not with what the future is, but with why the future may become what it becomes.

This way of thinking reminds me of ancient navigation.

An experienced captain cannot control the ocean, nor can he decide whether a storm will come. He cannot order ocean currents to change direction, nor can he make the monsoon arrive earlier or later. But he can understand currents, winds, and seasonal changes, and on that basis choose a route with a higher probability of success.

The meaning of navigation has never been to eliminate uncertainty. It is to find direction within uncertainty.

The development of civilization is the same.

We cannot control every variable, but we can understand the relationships among variables. We cannot guarantee that we will always be right, but we can keep increasing the probability of making better choices. Driving forces shape the state of reality. Reality produces new feedback. Once feedback enters the validation system, it pushes us to relearn and revise our judgment. And the new learning, in turn, influences the next round of choices. The future gradually takes shape through this cycle of continuous correction and renewal.

Therefore, Possibility Computing is not a new form of prediction science.

It is more like a new cognitive capacity.

It acknowledges the uncertainty of the future, and it also acknowledges the limits of human cognition. At the same time, however, it still believes that through continuous observation, continuous validation, and continuous learning, we can gradually improve our ability to understand the future.

This is, in fact, a way of thinking that Chinese people can understand very naturally.

Chinese people have never truly believed in passive fate. We believe that planning lies with human beings, while fulfillment depends on the conditions of heaven. We believe that things can be achieved through human effort, and we also believe in moving with the larger momentum of circumstances. What truly matters is never to wait for the future to arrive, but to participate actively in creating it after understanding its patterns.

For this reason, what Possibility Computing ultimately discusses is not technology.

Technology is only a tool. Models are only tools. Artificial intelligence is also only a tool. They can help us see more possibilities, but they cannot replace humanity in making choices.

Because after all the calculations are complete, one unavoidable question will still remain.

If the future contains countless possibilities, then what kind of future is worth creating? What kind of future is worth protecting? What kind of future is worth leaving to the next generation?

At this point, the question no longer belongs only to climate science. It no longer belongs only to planning.

It begins to enter civilization itself.

And this is precisely the question that the next chapter must continue to answer.

Appendix A: ClimateOS Conceptual System
A.1 Definition of ClimateOS

ClimateOS, or Climate Operating System, is not a software system in the traditional sense, but an Environmental Cognition and Runtime Framework.

Its core goal is not to replace existing meteorological models, environmental models, or governance systems, but to establish a continuously operating closed loop among observation, cognition, validation, governance, and evolution.

The question ClimateOS tries to answer is:

In a world full of uncertainty, how can humans and artificial intelligence jointly understand environmental change and form an action capacity that continuously corrects itself?

A.2 Core Operating Logic of ClimateOS

ClimateOS consists of five basic layers:

Observation
↓
Cognition
↓
Validation
↓
Governance
↓
Evolution

Among them, observation is responsible for acquiring facts; cognition is responsible for forming understanding; validation is responsible for judging credibility; governance is responsible for taking action; and evolution is responsible for continuous learning.

The five layers together form: Observation → Cognition → Validation → Governance → Evolution

A continuously circulating system.

A.3 Difference Between ClimateOS and Traditional Systems
Traditional systems: Observation → Prediction → Decision-making
ClimateOS: Observation → Cognition → Validation → Decision-making → Learning

Its core innovation is that the Validation Layer is formally introduced into the process of Earth system governance.

A.4 Positioning of ClimateOS

ClimateOS is not:

A weather forecasting system;
A digital twin platform;
An AI Agent platform;
ESG management software.

ClimateOS is: Earth System Validation Runtime.

An Earth system validation and runtime framework.

Its role is to coordinate continuous validation and collaborative operation among different models, different data sources, and different intelligent agents.

A.5 Long-term Goal of ClimateOS

Short term: an auxiliary framework for environmental governance.

Medium term: an Earth system validation platform.

Long term: EarthOS, an operating framework for Earth civilization.
