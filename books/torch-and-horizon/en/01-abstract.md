## Abstract

Over the past century, humanity has built increasingly powerful systems for observing and predicting the Earth.

From meteorological stations and remote-sensing satellites to numerical weather prediction, Earth system models, digital twins, and artificial intelligence, humanity’s ability to simulate the future is growing at an unprecedented speed.

However, since entering the twenty-first century, the core problem we face has begun to change.

The problem is no longer simply a lack of data, nor merely a lack of models. Rather, when more and more data, models, digital twins, and artificial intelligence systems exist at the same time, how can humanity judge which results are worthy of trust, and what actions should be taken based on them?

This book argues that the key to the future development of Earth system intelligence is not only to predict the future, but more importantly, to validate the future.

Based on the author’s more than twenty years of practice and reflection in environmental planning, landscape design, regional development, ecological governance, and climate adaptation, and combined with recent explorations of ClimateOS, CCZPS — the Computable Climate Zoning Planning System — Validation Runtime, and Possibility Computing, this book attempts to construct a new cognitive framework.

This framework does not attempt to build a larger Earth model, nor does it seek to replace existing climate prediction systems. Instead, it adds a layer of continuous validation, continuous correction, and continuous learning between observation, prediction, digital twins, and governance decisions.

This book proposes that the core of future governance capacity lies not in possessing more models, but in establishing validation models; not in obtaining more information, but in forming trusted cognition; not in pursuing a single answer, but in understanding the relationships among multiple possible futures.

Therefore, ClimateOS is not merely a technical system, but also an environmental cognitive framework. CCZPS is not merely a planning tool, but also a method for understanding the complex relationships among environment, society, and civilization.

From climate change to ecological restoration, from the dual-carbon transition to artificial intelligence, from environmental governance to Earth system governance, this book attempts to answer one question that runs through all of its discussions:

When the future becomes increasingly uncertain, how can humanity maintain a sense of direction and continue to create futures worth reaching?

This is both the starting point of ClimateOS and the central proposition toward which all discussions in this book ultimately point.

Why Am I Writing This Book?

Many friends have asked me the same question:

Why spend so many years thinking about a question that seems to have almost no end?

To be honest, I do not have a grand answer.

I am not a climate scientist, nor am I an artificial intelligence expert. I am certainly not someone who can decide the direction of global governance. I am simply a practitioner who has long worked in environmental planning, ecological construction, and regional development. Over the past twenty-plus years, I have worked on cities, villages, industrial parks, and river basins. I have also traveled through the deserts of northwestern China, the coastal cities of eastern China, the arid regions of the American West, and the vast landscapes of Australia.

These experiences did not give me answers directly. Instead, they kept bringing me back to the same question.

Why can some places prosper over the long term, while others gradually decline? Why can the same method succeed in one place, yet fail in another? Why does humanity possess increasingly advanced technologies, yet still face similar environmental and development dilemmas again and again?

I have increasingly come to realize that what is truly worth understanding may not be a particular technology or a particular policy, but the long-existing connections among environment, resources, society, and civilization — connections that are often overlooked.

Many people see this generation of Chinese people who have gone out into the world as part of globalization.

I did indeed leave China against the backdrop of that era, continuing my studies, work, and life overseas.

But I increasingly feel that this experience cannot simply be summarized as “immigration.”

If we take a longer view of time, I would rather see myself as an ordinary member of the countless Chinese explorers who, since modern times, have gone out into the world.

More than a century ago, many people crossed the oceans in search of paths toward modern science, industry, and education. After reform and opening up, generation after generation of Chinese people went out into the world, learning and communicating across different civilizations, while also rediscovering China and the world.

I am only a very ordinary one among them.

After coming overseas, I certainly saw many things worth learning from. Mature institutions, long-accumulated scientific traditions, and open international cooperation all benefited me greatly.

At the same time, however, I gradually discovered that today’s world is no longer merely a question of “whom we should learn from.”

Every civilization has its own achievements, and every civilization has its own limitations. Developed countries have not solved all problems, and China still has many areas that require continued exploration. What truly matters is not who replaces whom, but who can continue learning in a new era.

These experiences have made me increasingly believe that I did not come overseas to search for a ready-made answer.

I am more like someone who keeps comparing, observing, and validating across different civilizations, hoping to understand those patterns that remain true across cultures, countries, and eras.

Western science helped me understand the important value of analysis, validation, and professional specialization. At the same time, the holistic thinking, relational thinking, and long-formed idea of interdependence between humanity and nature in Eastern traditions have also led me to reconsider whether there may be another possibility for the development of civilization.

Later, I gradually discovered that what I was truly seeking was not the so-called “scriptures from the West.”

What I truly brought back was not a particular technology, nor a particular institution.

Rather, it was the ability to raise new questions again, through the collision and comparison of different civilizations.

Perhaps what humanity truly needs is not for one civilization to defeat another, nor a new form of zero-sum competition. Instead, as we jointly face the environment, resources, artificial intelligence, and the future, we need to reconnect the wisdom that different civilizations have accumulated over long periods of time.

After coming to Australia, this line of thought did not stop.

I have participated in planning and design, and I have also done ordinary physical labor. I have worked in a timber mill, assisted with teaching in schools, and continued to take part in community and environment-related practices. During the day, I face real life; at night, I continue revising this book. This contrast has not made me give up thinking. On the contrary, it has made me believe even more that a truly valuable idea must be able to return to ordinary people’s lives and face the real world, rather than remain only on paper.

In recent years, the development of artificial intelligence has given me new inspiration.

In the past, knowledge that took humanity decades to accumulate is now being connected at an unprecedented speed. I have begun to realize that we may be standing at the threshold of a new era. What will truly matter in the future is not merely making artificial intelligence smarter, but helping humanity and artificial intelligence jointly build a capacity for continuous observation, continuous validation, and continuous learning.

This is exactly why I wrote The Torch and the Horizon.

I do not believe that ClimateOS is already mature, nor do I believe that every view in this book has already been proven. On the contrary, it is more like an open record of thought, an exploration still in progress. I hope it can become a torch rather than a destination; a starting point for more discussion rather than the final answer.

If, one day, many of the ideas in this book are proven wrong, I will also be happy. Because what truly matters has never been the defense of a particular theory, but humanity’s continued movement toward reality.

Perhaps, decades from now, people will not remember ClimateOS, nor will they remember my name.

But if people in the future can understand the environment better than we do today, understand one another better, and understand the Earth we share more deeply; if modern science, artificial intelligence, and the long-accumulated wisdom of different civilizations can form new connections because of this era, then this book will have fulfilled its mission.

This book is not written for those who already have answers today. It is written for those who are still willing to keep asking questions.

Because the true torch of civilization is not the answer, but the courage to continue searching for answers.

The horizon represents a future not yet reached. The torch represents the questioning consciousness and truth-seeking spirit that continue to illuminate the road ahead.
