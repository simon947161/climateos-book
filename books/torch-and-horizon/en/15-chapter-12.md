## Chapter 12: Why Civilization Is Worth Continuing

By this point, I have become increasingly unwilling to discuss one question: what exactly is humanity?

Because the question is too vast.

For thousands of years, countless philosophers, scientists, and religious thinkers have tried to answer it. Some believe that humanity is the creation of God. Some believe that humanity is merely an accidental outcome of evolution. Some believe that humanity will eventually be replaced by a higher form of intelligence. Different civilizations have offered different answers, but even today, we still do not have an explanation that can satisfy everyone.

After living and working in different countries over these years, I have slowly come to feel that rather than asking what humanity is, perhaps the more important question is this: why is humanity worth existing?

When I was young, I once thought civilization meant cities. Later, after entering planning and design, I thought civilization meant roads, squares, parks, and buildings. Later still, when I went to Xinjiang and saw the people living on the edge of the desert, I began to feel that civilization might be something else. Those people were not wealthy. Their lives were often difficult. And yet they were still willing to plant trees, build canals, and construct homes amid wind and sand, devoting their limited lives to a cause whose results might only be seen decades later.

It was then that I first realized that the most precious thing in human civilization may not be how much wealth it creates, but its willingness to work toward a goal longer than one’s own lifetime.

Later, I went to the United States and saw the most prosperous side of the age of globalization. Vast supply chains connected every corner of the world. Countless goods crossed oceans and continents to gather in the same market. People believed that technology would solve problems, that capital could create the future, and that everyone could have a better life. But at the same time, I also saw another reality. Wealth was concentrating, resources were concentrating, opportunities were concentrating, and many people did not truly share in the prosperity that globalization had promised.

Later again, after coming to Australia, I saw another state of civilization. This is a country with abundant resources, stable institutions, and a relatively comfortable living environment. Many people no longer live under the anxiety of survival, and instead begin to think about the meaning of life itself. People here value fairness, rules, and the relationship between human beings and nature. Yet I also saw another problem: when a society remains comfortable for a long time, it may gradually lose the impulse to continue exploring forward.

These experiences kept leading me back to one question: if wealth is not the answer, if power is not the answer, and if technology is not the answer, then what is the real reason civilization deserves to continue?

I once approached people of different religions with this question in mind: Buddhism, Daoism, Christianity, Islam, and even the traditional beliefs of Aboriginal Australians, in which all things possess spirit. They differ greatly from one another, but later I discovered that they seem to be trying to answer the same question: what is the relationship between human beings and the world?

Some entrust the answer to God.

Some entrust the answer to nature.

Some entrust the answer to the afterlife.

Some entrust the answer to faith.

Although their forms of expression are different, they all strive to help human beings establish a meaning that reaches beyond the length of an individual life.

From this perspective, religion is not necessarily only theology. It is more like a way of preserving civilizational memory, a method through which individuals can connect themselves to a larger world.

And science is, in fact, much the same.

Science is not merely formulas and experiments. The essence of science is that it continuously revises itself. It allows error to exist, and it allows conclusions of the past to be overturned by the future. Precisely because of this, science can continue moving forward.

One day, I suddenly realized that although religion and science seem completely different, they share one thing in common: both are attempts to restrain the limitations of humanity itself.

Because human beings are not perfect.

We can be greedy. We can be jealous. We can be fearful. We can wage war. We can harm one another for the sake of interest.

But at the same time, we can also feel compassion. We can cooperate. We can reflect. We can record history. We can try to make life better for the next generation.

The true preciousness of civilization lies precisely in this capacity to continuously revise itself.

Over these years, while studying ClimateOS, CCZPS, and validation systems, I have increasingly felt that humanity’s most important ability may not be the ability to create tools, but the ability to create meaning.

Machines can calculate.

AI can learn.

In the future, it may even become far more intelligent than we are.

But at least up to this day, things such as homesickness, responsibility, sacrifice, goodwill, and trust still mainly exist within human civilization.

We work hard for our families. We offer help to our friends. We persist for many years for an ideal. We may even continue forward for a goal that may never be completed.

From the perspective of efficiency, such actions often seem meaningless. But from the perspective of civilization, they are precisely what civilization is made of.

For this reason, I have become increasingly unwilling to regard humanity as the master of the Earth. Nor do I wish to simply regard humanity as a parasite upon the Earth.

Humanity is more like a form of life capable of self-reflection.

We can create, and we can destroy. We can bring progress, and we can produce catastrophe.

Yet precisely because we can become aware of our mistakes, reflect upon our behavior, and attempt to make the world better, civilization possesses the value of continuing to exist. Perhaps one day in the future, human beings will achieve longevity, or even some form of immortality. Perhaps AI will become a new intelligent subject. Perhaps we will leave Earth and journey into farther interstellar space.

But no matter how the future changes, I have always believed that what a civilization ultimately leaves behind is not how much wealth it has created, nor how much technology it has mastered, but whether it has preserved goodwill toward life, toward the world, and toward the future.

Because civilization is worth continuing not because it is powerful enough.

It is worth continuing because it is still willing to become better.
