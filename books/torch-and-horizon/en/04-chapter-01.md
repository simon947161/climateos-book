## Chapter 1: Why Do Some Systems Fall Out of Balance?
— From Extreme Weather to the Vulnerability of Civilization

This summer, I saw news reports about a series of extreme weather events across China. Northeastern China experienced unusually low temperatures and severe storms, while parts of southern China suffered from persistent heat waves and heavy rainfall. For people today, extreme weather no longer seems unfamiliar. Yet when these phenomena appear in the news more and more frequently, I still cannot help but pause and think. Low temperatures in the Northeast, droughts in inland regions, heavy rainfall along the coast, bushfires in Australia, heat waves in Europe, and the constant fluctuations in food prices, energy supply, and insurance costs over recent years may appear to belong to different countries and different fields. Yet at some level, they seem to be connected.

Many people group these phenomena under the term climate change. But I increasingly feel that climate change may only be the surface expression. The deeper problem may come from the system itself.

This line of thought did not begin recently. Looking back, I seem to have been asking this question for more than twenty years. When I was young, I did not know what I was looking for. I thought I was studying design. Later, I thought I was studying cities. Later still, I thought I was studying ecology and climate. Only after many years did I slowly realize that what I had been observing all along was how systems form, and why they fall out of balance.

In 2005, I moved from Shanghai to Shenzhen for work. At that time, Shenzhen was developing at a very rapid pace. The whole industry was learning from southern experience, and many design methods were regarded as generally applicable. But very soon, I discovered a very real contradiction in project work: the same design scheme could work in the South, yet might not work at all in the North.

At that time, we had already clearly proposed the concept of “north-south differences.” Plants were different. Spaces were different. Climate was different. Even people’s habits of use and ways of life were different. Many small gardens and small-scale landscapes that could be easily realized in Shenzhen produced completely different effects when placed in northern China. It was then that I began to realize that a method successful in one place could not necessarily be copied to another. The environment itself has its own logic, and the first responsibility of a designer is to respect that logic.

Looking back today, this may seem like only a professional and technical issue. But at the time, it had already planted a line of thought that would influence me for many years afterward. If plants change because of the environment, and if design methods fail because of regional conditions, then could cities, industries, and even civilizations also face similar problems? Could some of the success stories we take for granted only apply under specific conditions, and therefore cannot be replicated without limit?

Around 2008, I had returned to Shanghai and begun participating in larger-scale municipal projects and work related to the World Expo. It was also during that period that I first truly encountered the issue of climate change. Discussions about sea-level rise, extreme weather, and global warming were becoming more frequent, and many experts were predicting the risks that might appear in the future.

To be honest, at that time I did not believe I could solve a problem as vast as climate change. I simply felt that if this was a challenge all of humanity would face over the coming decades, then someone should try to understand it, study it, and seek an entry point from the field they knew best. I have always believed that major problems do not necessarily have to be solved from the center; breakthroughs may also emerge from the margins.

So I began to think about another question. If sea levels really continued to rise, what would happen inside the Eurasian continent? When coastal cities came under pressure, would inland regions face new opportunities and new problems? With these questions in mind, I turned my attention to Xinjiang, to those arid regions that rarely receive attention from the mainstream perspective.

Later, I went to Xinjiang. What attracted me at first was not the project itself, but the question. I wanted to know what would happen, under the broader background of climate change, to those regions far from the ocean and far from mainstream attention. Many people were focused on coastlines, but I wanted to look at the center of the continent. Many people were discussing future risks, but I wanted to look at future possibilities.

There, I participated in planning research in places such as Karamay, and I also came into contact with many materials on arid-region ecosystems. The research findings of the Institute of Arid Ecology at Xinjiang University gave me great inspiration. For the first time, I systematically learned the causes of saline-alkali land formation and began to understand the complex relationships among precipitation, evaporation, groundwater, and soil. Many things that had been taken for granted in coastal areas became entirely different problems in arid regions.

It was then that I began to realize that many so-called development problems are, first of all, environmental problems. Why does land degrade? Why does vegetation disappear? Why do rivers change course? These changes do not happen suddenly on a single day. They accumulate little by little over decades, or even over centuries. The truly dangerous part of a system often does not lie in sudden collapse, but in the fact that when it has already begun to fall out of balance, people still think everything is normal.

In search of more answers, I later went to the United States.

Many Chinese people come to know America through New York, Wall Street, or Silicon Valley. But what interested me most were the arid regions of the American West. I went to Arizona, Nevada, and California, and I also observed the development patterns around Los Angeles and Las Vegas.

There, I truly felt for the first time the shaping power of infrastructure in the development of civilization. Many Chinese cities are accustomed to expanding outward from a central urban core, with ring roads growing outward one after another. Many cities on the American West Coast, however, unfold along transportation corridors, water resources, and mountain boundaries, forming a pattern of linear development. Where there is a stable water source, communities are more likely to form. Where ecological conditions are better, wealth is more likely to accumulate. Behind many issues that appear to be economic, there are still ecological and resource-related questions.

My trip to the United States led me to think about another question. Where does the prosperity of a civilization truly come from? Does it come from capital? From institutions? From technology? Or from those deeper natural conditions and infrastructures that are often overlooked? I had no answers to these questions at the time, but they stayed in my mind.

In 2015, I came to Australia.

If the United States allowed me to see how resources support the development of a civilization, Australia allowed me to see another challenge faced by a successful civilization. This country has abundant mineral resources, stable social institutions, and a relatively high standard of living. Many people long for such a life, and I did indeed see aspects of this country that deserve respect.

At the same time, however, I also saw a kind of inertia. The comfort brought by resources made many people believe that their existing way of life could continue for a long time. Mining, energy, and export industries support the functioning of the economy. People enjoy a high quality of life, yet without realizing it, they may also lose part of the motivation to change the future.

Once, I discussed future resource issues with an Australian scientist. He told me that, according to existing research, Australia’s underground water reserves would be sufficient for humanity to use for thousands of years. His judgment was not wrong, but I did not immediately feel optimistic.

I suddenly thought of the film City of Ember. In that story, humanity is forced to move underground because the surface environment has deteriorated. More than two hundred years later, people have even forgotten that they once came from the surface. So I asked him: if we can keep the surface ecosystem healthy, keep rivers flowing, keep forests growing, and allow people to continue living under the sunlight, then why must we go underground to live?

For many years afterward, I remembered that conversation. Because I discovered that when human beings face environmental problems, the discussion is often about whether resources are sufficient. But what I care about more is another question: what kind of future do we ultimately hope to build?

Only many years later, when I looked back again, did I realize that what I had been asking was not really about weather, nor only about climate change. What I truly wanted to understand was why some systems can maintain vitality over the long term, while others slowly move toward imbalance.

The storms in Harbin, the plants in Shenzhen, the saline-alkali land in Xinjiang, the infrastructure of the United States, and the resource society of Australia may seem unrelated. Yet through these years of observation, they gradually converged into the same question.

Why can some systems continue to evolve, while others gradually fall out of balance?

And this is a question I am still asking today.

I will write this chapter with great restraint.

Because this is not an “author’s autobiography,” but an explanation to readers of why this question has been worth more than twenty years of continued inquiry.
