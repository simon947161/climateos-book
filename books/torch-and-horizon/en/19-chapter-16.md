## Chapter 16: What Kind of Future Do We Truly Hope to Create?
— From ClimateOS to EarthOS

At this point, I increasingly feel that ClimateOS is not the true destination of this book.

It is only an entrance.

Over these years, I have kept speaking about climate, environment, prediction, validation, and possibility computing. On the surface, it may seem that I have been discussing technologies and methods. But the deeper I go, the more I realize that what I truly want to ask is not “Can we predict the future?” Nor is it “Can we govern the climate?” It is another question, simpler and heavier at the same time:

What kind of future do we truly hope to create?

A human life is short.

So short that many ideals have not yet truly unfolded before a person has already begun to grow old. So short that just as many things are beginning to become clear, the body and reality no longer allow one to keep charging forward.

Many people, when young, believe they can change the world. Later, slowly, they learn to compromise, to grow quiet, and to shrink their dreams into a size that life can bear.

I do not wish to criticize this choice.

Because life itself is already difficult.

A person must support a family, work, care for parents and children, and preserve a little steadiness of their own amid the current of the age. Not everyone should be a hero, and not everyone must sacrifice themselves for a distant ideal.

Yet civilization continues precisely because there are always some people willing to take one more step.

Some build roads. Some plant trees. Some teach. Some study science. Some guard the land. Some devote their entire lives to a cause whose results they may never live to see. They may not be remembered, and they may not achieve success in any worldly sense, but what they leave behind continues to grow in the lives of those who come later.

When we were children, we admired heroes.

Huang Jiguang, Dong Cunrui, and those who gave their lives for an ideal once deeply shaped the spiritual world of a generation.

Later, as I grew older, I slowly came to understand that what truly supports the progress of civilization may not be heroes alone.

There is also another kind of person: those who know they may not succeed, yet still continue to try; those who know they may be misunderstood, yet still continue to explore; those who know they may never see the result, yet still choose to pass the torch to those who come after them.

I increasingly feel that the greatest capacity of human civilization may not be creation, but transmission.

Clay tablets were transmission. Bamboo slips were transmission. Stone inscriptions were transmission. Books were transmission. Religion was transmission. Science, too, was transmission.

Today, code, data, artificial intelligence, and digital systems have also become new forms of transmission.

The forms keep changing, but humanity has always been doing the same thing:

refusing to let what truly matters be forgotten.

So what is most worth transmitting?

Is it wealth?

Wealth will be redistributed.

Is it power?

Power will keep changing hands.

Is it technology?

Technology will eventually be replaced by newer technology.

What is truly worth transmitting may be a way of understanding the world, a willingness to correct oneself when facing error, and a civilizational attitude that still holds goodwill toward life, toward land, and toward the future.

Over these years, I have traveled through many places.

From Xinjiang to Shanghai, from the United States to Australia, from cities to deserts, from rivers to coastlines.

I once thought I was studying environmental problems. Later, I discovered that what I was studying was the problem of civilization.

Because behind all environmental problems, everything eventually returns to the same question:

How should life continue to exist?

If there truly is a civilization in the future that is worth creating, then it must first answer an older question:

How should we treat other forms of life? How should we treat land, water, forests, deserts, and oceans?

Humanity cannot decide that a desert must no longer be a desert. Nor can humanity decide that the ocean must become land.

Nature has its own boundaries, its own order, and its own rhythms.

The real question is not whether humanity can transform nature, but whether we know how far we may transform it, and where we must stop.

The ancient Chinese said:

Heaven has the virtue of cherishing life.

This sentence is not merely a moral exhortation. It contains a deep ecological intuition.

All things between heaven and earth live together. The greater human ability becomes, the less we can afford to act only according to desire.

We may build. We may create. We may change the environment. But we must not forget that all life bears the consequences within the same system.

I once imagined a simple scene.

If a group of penguins fell into an ice pit, then according to certain strict principles of nature protection, perhaps humans should not intervene.

But when a person truly stands there, sees life struggling, and sees that they are only a few steps away from being able to climb out, compassion breaks through cold rules.

In the end, people cut a small path in the ice, allowing the penguins to walk out step by step.

It is a small thing.

But it reveals something at the deepest level of civilization.

We are not only observers. We are beings who can be moved by life.

Australia has made me return to this question again and again over the years.

It is like a continental-scale laboratory.

Here there are vast arid regions, bushfires, a mining economy, ecological conservation, and many challenges brought by climate change.

It possesses a highly mature rule-based system of modern society, while also exposing many contradictions of a resource-based economy.

It is less like an answer, and more like an experiment.

A large experiment in how future civilization might coexist with the environment.

China also faces its own problems.

Drought, floods, energy transition, ecological restoration, population change, industrial upgrading.

Different countries have different paths, but many of their problems are, in fact, connected.

Air does not stop flowing because of national borders.

Rivers do not change direction because of institutions.

Climate change does not ask where people come from.

When we stretch the scale of time and enlarge the scale of space, we discover that many problems that appear independent eventually converge within the same Earth system.

This is why I am increasingly unwilling to understand ClimateOS as a simple software system.

If it truly has meaning, then it should become a way of cognition.

A way to help people understand the environment, understand feedback, understand responsibility, and understand the future.

For many years, we have believed that humanity will one day go to Mars.

Some people describe Martian cities in a grand voice, while others quietly advance deep-space exploration.

These things are exciting, because they represent civilization’s courage to move toward the unknown.

But when we seriously think about the future, one question can never be avoided:

If one day we really leave Earth, what kind of world will we build there?

If we merely carry the greed, short-sightedness, and division of Earth to another planet, then what meaning does interstellar travel have?

If a civilization cannot coexist with its mother planet, then it will also find it difficult to truly coexist with a new world.

This is why I increasingly feel that before reaching for the sea of stars, humanity must first learn how to live with its own home.

Earth is not a background.

It is the mother body of the civilization we know.

What ClimateOS must ultimately move toward may not be ClimateOS itself.

It may be EarthOS.

Not a piece of software. Not a platform. Not a database. But a foundational setting for Earth civilization.

It reminds us:

Development must coexist with the environment;
Technology must be subject to validation;
Capability must be restrained;
Civilization must learn to revise itself.

At this point, someone may ask: what do these things have to do with ordinary people?

After all, not everyone studies climate. Not everyone studies planning. Not everyone cares about Earth systems, artificial intelligence, or future civilization.

Most people still face family, work, income, and life itself every day.

But precisely because of this, I increasingly feel that a truly valuable system of the future should not be only a tool for experts. It should become a tool that ordinary people can participate in.

If a system can only be understood by a small number of people, then it remains only knowledge.

If it can help more people find their own position, then it has a chance to become part of civilization.

What I have been trying to do over these years — whether through ClimateOS, CCZPS, or later EcoChain — is, in fact, the same thing:

To help different people see the relationship between themselves and the environment, between themselves and society, and between themselves and the future.

Farmers care about land. Engineers care about construction. Enterprises care about operations. Teachers care about education. Scientists care about patterns. Administrators care about order.

On the surface, everyone is doing something different.

But when time becomes long enough, and space becomes broad enough, all these things eventually converge within the same system.

In the past, we lacked the ability to see all these relationships at the same time.

Today, artificial intelligence has made this possible for the first time.

It can help us understand complex relationships. It can help us discover connections that were once overlooked.

I do not believe that artificial intelligence will replace humanity.

I would rather understand it as an amplifier.

It amplifies our knowledge, and it also amplifies our bias. It amplifies our creativity, and it also amplifies our mistakes.

Therefore, what truly matters in the future is not how intelligent machines become, but whether humanity knows where it hopes to go.

If I had to summarize these years of reflection in one sentence, I would write it this way:

Understand the environment, recognize the patterns; protect life, and create the future together.

These sixteen Chinese characters are not a slogan.

They are a reminder.

A reminder not to forget where we came from;

A reminder not to forget the land beneath our feet;

A reminder not to forget that the future does not belong to one country, one organization, or one technology, but to all those who are willing to build it.

Perhaps future humanity will possess abilities far greater than those we have today.

Perhaps artificial intelligence will help us solve many problems that today remain beyond our reach.

Perhaps civilization will eventually move toward the farther sea of stars.

But no matter where we go, I still hope one thing can be preserved.

That humanity once faced this world with goodwill, and tried to make it better.

ClimateOS is not the answer.

CCZPS is not the answer.

EarthOS will not be the answer either.

They are only one effort by the people of this era to understand Earth again, to understand the future again, and to understand our own responsibility again.

When people in the future look back on this age, perhaps they will already possess tools far more advanced than ours.

But I still hope they will remember:

Before artificial intelligence and interstellar civilization arrived, there was once a generation of people who seriously thought about the simplest question —

How should we live with our own planet?

And this question may be the true starting point of all future civilizations.

What Exactly Is the Relationship Between ClimateOS, CCZPS, and EarthOS?

At this point, many friends may share the same question: What is ClimateOS? What is CCZPS? What is Validation Runtime? What is EcoChain? And why do these concepts appear one after another?

For someone encountering these ideas for the first time, it is easy to feel that they are separate from one another, or even that they are simply new terms emerging one after another. But in fact, they are not different systems. They are the natural extension of the same question at different levels.

To understand the relationship among them is like understanding the relationship among the roots, trunk, branches, leaves, and fruit of a tree. They all come from the same living body, but each carries a different function.

ClimateOS was not originally conceived as a piece of software.

It is more like an environmental cognitive framework — a way to help humanity understand the environment, understand change, and understand its own position within that change.

Many governance systems in the past emphasized observation, prediction, and decision-making. But I have increasingly found that merely possessing data and models cannot solve the problem, because what is truly missing is an understanding of the whole set of relationships within the environment.

Therefore, the question ClimateOS tries to answer is not “What will happen in the future?”

Rather, it asks: “How do we understand what is happening now, and where do we stand within it?”

In this sense, ClimateOS is closer to a cognitive operating system than to a technical platform.

Once cognition has been established, a new question immediately appears.

Understanding reality is one thing. But the future is never singular.

A region may move toward recovery, or it may move toward degradation. A policy may bring development, or it may create new problems. If the future contains multiple possibilities, then how should we compare these possibilities? How should we judge which path is more worth trying?

CCZPS emerged from this background.

It was first defined as the Computable Climate Zoning Planning System. But as the thinking deepened, it gradually moved beyond climate zoning itself and evolved into a framework for possibility computing.

What it does is organize the many forces, constraints, and trends of change within complex environments, helping us explore the relationships among different futures and understand how those futures come into being.

Yet the more possibilities the future contains, the more obvious the next difficulty becomes.

Different models may produce different results. Different experts may offer different judgments. Different artificial intelligences may even form different reasoning paths.

When multiple futures exist at the same time, what should we trust?

At this point, Validation Runtime — the validation operating layer — becomes important.

It no longer focuses only on prediction itself, but on whether a prediction is trustworthy. It no longer focuses only on the answer itself, but on whether the evidence is sufficient. It no longer focuses only on the result itself, but on whether the process by which that result was formed can withstand validation.

It is like a mechanism for continuously calibrating direction. It constantly compares the distance between reality and hypothesis, and through feedback it revises cognition, allowing the whole system to develop a capacity for continuous learning.

If learning produces experience, then how that experience is preserved becomes another unavoidable question.

The greatest loss of civilization is often not making mistakes, but repeating the same mistakes again and again.

EcoChain was proposed for this reason.

It is not merely a chain in the technical sense. It is a mechanism of civilizational memory, used to record the processes of observation, validation, practice, and contribution.

It tries to settle and preserve those experiences that truly promote environmental improvement, social learning, and systemic progress, so that they can be carried forward across individuals, organizations, and even generations.

Validation is responsible for learning.

EcoChain is responsible for memory.

Together, they form the foundation for the continuous evolution of civilization.

When cognition, possibility, validation, and memory gradually become connected, a larger question naturally emerges.

If these mechanisms do not apply only to a single city, a single watershed, or a single country, but can be extended to the entire Earth system, then what will they ultimately become?

EarthOS appears precisely from this line of thought.

It is not another independent system. It is the natural result of ClimateOS extending toward a larger scale.

ClimateOS focuses on the relationship between humanity and the environment.

EarthOS focuses on the overall operating state of the Earth, formed together by life, resources, civilization, energy, and artificial intelligence.

The question it faces is no longer only environmental governance. It is how the Earth system can operate with long-term stability, and how different civilizations can coexist and evolve together on the same planet.

Therefore, ClimateOS, CCZPS, Validation Runtime, EcoChain, and EarthOS are not five independent projects.

They are different links along one continuous chain of logic.

ClimateOS is responsible for establishing cognition.

CCZPS is responsible for exploring possibility.

Validation Runtime is responsible for validating judgment.

EcoChain is responsible for preserving experience.

EarthOS is responsible for extending all of this to the entire Earth system.

The reason these concepts appear one after another is not that we need to keep inventing more and more new names.

It is that each time one question is answered, a new question naturally appears.

In the end, they are all trying to answer the same proposition:

As the future becomes more complex and more uncertain, how can humanity understand reality, explore possibility, validate direction, accumulate experience, and ultimately create a future worth reaching?

Why would someone, in 2026, still spend so much energy building a foundational framework that does not seem able to make money immediately?

And this question, in fact, is not only my personal question. It is precisely the question shared by all foundational researchers in the age of AI.
